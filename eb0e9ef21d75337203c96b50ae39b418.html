Pastor apologizes for holding worship amid shutdowns

A Westmoreland County church defiantly met for worship on Sunday at a time when houses of worship shuttered over the weekend to comply with health officials’ urgent calls for social distancing to prevent the spread of the coronavirus.

Now in the face of public backlash, the pastor is apologizing on a Facebook post, even as he maintains belief that the Bible promises believers that “no plague will come near your home.”

The church will do livestream-only services for the foreseeable future, he now says.

Word of Life Church in Hempfield Township removed a video of its Sunday service from its website. But excerpts aired on WTAE-TV show the pastor, Tom Walters, denouncing the urgings of the Centers for Disease Control and Pennsylvania health officials to avoid large gatherings.

"I don’t want anyone telling us we can’t worship our God collectively as a body," Pastor Walters said in the Sunday service. "This could possibly be, disguised in everything else, a direct attack against the church itself."

Commenters on the church’s Facebook page angrily criticized the decision as putting at risk the health and lives of church members and those they come in contact with.

Pastor Walters said on Facebook Monday that he and the staff did not ask anyone to attend but opened the doors to those who wanted to join in the service.

In his Facebook apology on Monday, he maintained “it was not out of arrogance or defiance, but solely for the purpose of praying for our churches, communities and nation” that the church opened.

He cited a biblical Psalm as saying “If you make the Lord your refuge, ... no plague will come near your home.”

He said his “heart was so heavy to experience” what he depicted as “hateful comments.”

“I will try harder to be more of an asset to our community,” he said. “Please do not blame our people for this past Sunday, they were simply trying to honor me, and more so our Lord!”

Local houses of worship of all types, including Buddhist, Christian, Hindu, Jewish and Muslim, closed or livestreamed services this past weekend during the crisis.
