Feds portray arrested Troy Hill man as obsessed with mass shootings

An unemployed Troy Hill felon portrayed by the government as being obsessed with mass shootings and Nazis was ordered detained Friday pending trial on a charge that he illegally possessed an assault-style gun.

Dennis A. Riggs, 50, had seven guns, ammunition and numerous bladed weapons in his house, according to testimony in U.S. District Court by an FBI agent working with a domestic terrorism task force.

Riggs is barred from owning guns and ammunition because of a 1994 conviction in Allegheny County for aggravated assault, a felony.

Agent Troy Jeffers described the decor of Riggs’ Hatteras Street home as having a Nazi theme, with framed portraits throughout his house of Adolf Hitler, the Nazi eagle symbol and images of swastikas.

Agents executing a search warrant Wednesday as part of an ongoing investigation found the guns, ammunition and what Magistrate Judge Lisa Pupo Lenihan described as “excessive” amounts of alcohol.

A search of Riggs’ cellphone turned up video of him — played in court — in a shirt with a swastika emblem loading an assault-style rifle and giving the sieg heil Nazi salute.

Also on his phone, according to Agent Jeffers: the live-streamed video of the March 2019 mass shooting at a Christchurch, New Zealand, mosque and a picture of Dylann Roof, the mass shooter in the 2015 massacre at a Charleston, S.C., church, along with his federal prison contact information.

Numerous photos of Riggs holding guns were on the phone, including one of an AR-15 assault-style rifle lying on a Nazi banner and another of the gun atop camouflage pants with multiple rounds of ammunition strewn about.

As the court displayed the exhibits, Riggs — portly, bespectacled and wearing a red jail uniform — craned his neck to see.

Riggs has a valid passport and has traveled to the United Kingdom, Germany and Hungary — most recently tracking overseas in November.

The government asked for Riggs’ detention both because prosecutors believe that he poses a flight risk and is a danger to the community.

Assistant U.S. Attorney Jessica Smolar noted that Riggs was arrested in 2005 by Pittsburgh police for threatening a neighbor with a gun during a confrontation in which he used racial slurs.

In that incident, Pittsburgh police said Riggs announced that "he was ridding the Catholic neighborhood of blacks."

At the time, Riggs displayed a Nazi flag on his mantel and bragged to neighbors that his friends called him Adolf Hitler.

Riggs was involuntarily committed for psychiatric treatment and the charges — ethnic intimidation and simple assault — were dismissed.

In the current case, Riggs is accused of illegally obtaining the AR-15 in 2018 through a straw purchase at a Washington County gun shop by his significant other.

But the government, clearly, sees a bigger problem and is concerned that Riggs has all the makings of a potential mass shooter.

Thomas Livingston, a federal public defender, argued that the only thing his client had done with the gun since 2018 was to pose with it for videos. He also said that Riggs was under care of the Veterans Administration and acknowledged that his client had mental health, alcohol and medical issues.

Judge Lenihan said she did not need to hear the prosecution’s argument to hold Riggs.

Her mind was made up based on the evidence, including the “very concerning” 2005 arrest, the weapons, the “significant amount” of Nazi paraphernalia, his overseas travel, the videos “apparently paying homage to Adolf Hitler” — and the mass shooting imagery.
