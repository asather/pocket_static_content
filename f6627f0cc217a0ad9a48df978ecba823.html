Mail-in ballots could mean faster results when polls close in today's Pa. primary election

A significant chunk of election results could be available as soon as polls close in Tuesday’s primary election in Allegheny County, officials say.

This would be a departure from past elections, when counties weren’t permitted to start tallying their absentee ballots until at least 8 p.m.

Now they’re allowed to start counting absentees — and the thousands and thousands of mail-in ballots that were a new feature in this year’s contests as well — at 7 a.m., a task that Allegheny County is committing dozens of staffers to in a guarded warehouse.

If all goes as planned, when 8 p.m. hits, they’ll have tallied a majority of the more than 188,000 mail-in or absentee ballots they’ve received, and will be allowed to post those results right away, officials said — giving the public an immediate look at who’s winning all the races.

Results will be partial, though, and the final outcomes of races won’t be determined — in most cases — at least until in-person votes from Tuesday’s polling places are counted, which could take a few hours.

There could also be holdups in the dayside absentee and mail-in counting, officials acknowledged, which could levy unforeseen delays.

“We’ve never scanned 180,000 ballots here before,” elections division manager David Voye said Monday. “It may take a lot longer than we anticipate.”

“We have over 100 people working on this,” Mr. Voye added. “I don’t know what time we’ll finish, but we intend on staying here until we’re done.”

Another factor that could potentially delay the final outcomes in some races is that Allegheny County voters are now allowed to postmark their mail-in or absentee ballots on Tuesday and put them in the mail. Gov. Tom Wolf, by executive order on Monday, pushed back the deadline one week for the county — and five others — to count those ballots by mail.

While 188,000 voted ballots had been returned to Allegheny County as of Monday, more than 280,000 had applied.

Voters in Allegheny County can still submit their mail-in and absentee ballots in-person on Tuesday, if they choose, by dropping them off in the lobby of the county-office building at 542 Forbes Ave. between 7 a.m. and 8 p.m.

The county gave reporters a walkthrough of its ballot-counting operation on Monday, a multi-step process that includes using letter-opening machines to extract mail-in and absentee ballots from envelopes and high-capacity scanners to tally them.

Technicians will be on site in case there are issues with either machine type, said Chet Harhut, deputy manager of the elections division.

Mr. Voye said the county ran a computer-generated test of the ballot scanners — which are being used for the first time this election — with a test sample of 50,000 ballots.

“All ballots were tabulated correctly and matched the outcome we expected,” Mr. Voye said.

The test run took about four hours, and didn’t include the physical opening of envelopes, Mr. Voye said.

When polls close at 8 p.m., the warehouse also will begin to receive the locked ballot boxes from in-person polling places — hand-delivered by authorized officials. The only people who have the keys, officials said, are the ballot scanner operators.

Once batches of ballots are scanned and tallied, the machines print off a piece of paper indicating how many ballots were in that particular batch, what time it was printed and which machine they were scanned on, officials said.

The tallied ballots are then stored in a locked room.
