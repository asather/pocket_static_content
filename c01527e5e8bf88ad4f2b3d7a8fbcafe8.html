Mohawk beats Carlynton to earn first championship bid in school history

A red and white banner hangs high inside the Mohawk High School gymnasium honoring the school’s 1970 boys basketball team that made a WPIAL championship appearance. That was a long 50 years ago, but even today, there still isn’t a girls basketball banner to match it — something this season’s Mohawk squad has used as motivation over the course of its dominant 2019-20 run.

The Warriors moved closer toward earning a banner of their own Wednesday, knocking off Carlynton, 64-58, in the 3A semifinals to advance to the WPIAL championship for the first time in school history.

“We’ve been talking about [the championship banner] all year,” said Mohawk coach Mike O’Lare.

And now, they have a chance to put that talk to action.

“We knew we had the potential to [earn a championship bid],” junior Paige Julian said. “We just knew we had to work every day. That’s what we do, we continue to do it every day.”

Julian accounted for over half of Mohawk’s scoring total, tallying 26 of her 33 points in the final two quarters as the Warriors staved off a late Carlynton comeback. She hit a trio of 3s and went 12 for 15 from the free throw line, while Nadia Lape finished with 17 points and Karly McCutcheon added seven.

Other semifinal

This season, matchups between section foes Beaver and Avonworth have been decided by the slimmest of margins. First came a two-point win for the Bobcats. Then came a three-point win for the Antelopes. So when round three of the series came Wednesday night, not a single possession could be taken for granted.

Nor a single free throw.

Beaver junior Emma Pavelek hit 11 of her 12 attempts at the charity stripe in a tight fourth quarter to push the Bobcats past Avonworth, 46-37, for the program’s second championship berth in the last three years. Pavelek finished with 25 points for the game, but it was her timely — and clutch — scoring down the stretch that ultimately mattered most.

“Every time I got fouled, I wasn’t even thinking about the shot per se,” Pavelek said after the game. “I was thinking more about that I couldn’t let my team down. I knew making those were going to be critical. In the back of my head, it was, ‘I can’t let my team down, I can’t let my team down.’”

Instead, she did the opposite.

“[If] she misses even a few of those, it’s a totally different game,” said Beaver coach Greg Huston. “... We do put a lot on Emma’s shoulders and expect a lot from her, but to be honest with you, she’s been doing that for the last two years.”
