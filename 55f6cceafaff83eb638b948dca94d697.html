U.S. dismisses Iraq’s request to work on a troop withdrawal plan

BAGHDAD — Iraq’s caretaker prime minister asked Washington to work out a road map for an American troop withdrawal, but the U.S. State Department on Friday bluntly rejected the request, saying the two sides should instead talk about how to “recommit” to their partnership.

Thousands of anti-government protesters turned out in the capital and southern Iraq, many calling on both Iran and America to leave Iraq, reflecting their anger and frustration over the two rivals trading blows on Iraqi soil.

The request from Prime Minister Adel Abdul-Mahdi pointed to his determination to push ahead with his demands for U.S. troops to leave Iraq, which were stoked by the American drone strike on Jan. 3 that killed top Iranian Gen. Qassem Soleimani.

In a phone call Thursday night, he told Secretary of State Mike Pompeo that recent U.S. actions were unacceptable breaches of Iraqi sovereignty and a violation of their security agreements, according to his office.

He asked Mr. Pompeo to “send delegates to Iraq to prepare a mechanism” to carry out the Iraqi Parliament’s resolution on withdrawing foreign troops, according to the statement.

“The prime minister said American forces had entered Iraq and drones are flying in its airspace without permission from Iraqi authorities, and this was a violation of the bilateral agreements,” the statement added.

Mr. Abdul-Mahdi signaled he was standing by the push for U.S. forces to leave despite signs of de-escalation by Tehran and Washington after Iran retaliated for Soleimani’s death by firing missiles that hit two Iraqi bases where American troops are based but caused no casualties.

Iraqis feel furious and helpless at being caught in the middle of the fighting.

Mr. Abdul-Mahdi has said he rejects all violations of Iraqi sovereignty, including both the Iranian and U.S. strikes.

The U.S. State Department flatly dismissed Mr. Abdul-Mahdi’s request, saying that U.S. troops are crucial for the fight against the Islamic State group and it would not discuss removing them from the region.

Mr. Pompeo indicated Friday the troops would remain, and he added that the U.S. would continue its mission to train Iraqi security forces in its quest to counter the Islamic State.
