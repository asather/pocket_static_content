Low ridership during COVID-19 paints a bleak financial picture for Port Authority

Port Authority’s chief finance officer told a board committee Thursday that the full effect of ridership cuts and rising expenses as a result of the COVID-19 virus won’t be known until at least May, but what he did say paints a bleak picture.

Pete Schenk told the committee during a video meeting the agency’s passenger revenue for last week was about $20,000. Under normal circumstances, the agency averages about $1.2 million a week in passenger revenue, which accounts for about 20% of its revenue.

Ridership on April 10, the most recent day available, showed the system carried 37,060 riders, down 83% from the same day in 2019. Passenger revenue for a month usually is between $7 million and $8 million — including students whose riders are paid by their school or college and weren’t part of last week’s figures — probably will be closer to $500,000 for April, Mr. Schenk said.

For March, which had about two weeks of ridership drops due to the novel coronavirus, overall revenue was down about $1 million, but Mr. Schenk said that was misleading because it included about $2 million in revenue from February.

“You’re definitely going to see [the financial implications] in the April statements,” Mr. Schenk said.

The agency said earlier this month it expects lost revenue and higher costs for cleaning supplies and overtime for cleaning crews that sanitize buses daily to reach $30 million by the end of the fiscal year June 30. 

The good news is the agency continues to be under budget for expenses for the year and has $153.7 million in reserve funds. Additionally, Congress has approved $125 billion in federal reimbursements for transit agencies as a result of the virus and Port Authority is eligible for $141.75 million.

Because of ridership declines, the agency reduced service by about 25% last month and to curb the spread of the virus has passengers entering from rear doors. That means cash customers ride for free.
