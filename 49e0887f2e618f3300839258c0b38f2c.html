Joe Starkey’s Mailbag: Is Colin Moran a trade waiting to happen or part of the Pirates’ future?

Welcome to Joe Starkey’s mailbag, where the Post-Gazette columnist and 93.7 The Fan radio host answers your questions about sports, life, Lee Lacy and everything in between. If you want to ask Joe a question, tweet him @JoeStarkey1 or email him at jstarkey@post-gazette.com. On to the questions, which refreshingly were laced with Pirate rage after Monday’s home opener …

Todd McCollum, @mccollumT: Can we at least say that Colin Moran is and will be a viable and component major league hitter?

Starkey: I think we can, Dr. McCollum. Question is, what’s his future here? He turns 28 in October, and yet he’s only in his third full major league season. What if he goes from competent hitter — .275 career average with improving power — to very good one? The Pirates should always be in the market for those, and they have control over Moran for the next three years.

Sometimes players develop late. Consider the case of Brandon Moss, who was 28 when he wound up in Oakland. He’d been a disappointment with the Pirates for a few seasons, but he suddenly took off, bashing 76 home runs over the next three seasons with the A’s. Sometimes, all a guy needs is a full-time chance and some patience.

The complication here, of course, is Ke’Bryan Hayes — an enticing third-base prospect who must be given a chance to develop ASAP. With no minor leagues at the moment, there is nowhere but the majors for him to develop. Hayes (currently sidelined) already is an elite fielder.

So, what do the Pirates do with Moran if he continues to bash the baseball?

Trade him, would be one answer. His stock might never be higher. His price is soon to go up.

Keep him, would be the other, and be very thankful the National League has a designated hitter this season. It won’t have one next year but is sure to adopt it full time in 2022. The Pirates, if they play Hayes, might have to get creative with Moran the rest of this season and especially next season. He has played a bit at first and second and even some in the outfield (not recommended).

It also might come to pass — who knows? — that Moran replaces Josh Bell at first base if the Pirates trade Bell this offseason.

Meantime, if someone sees Moran as a piece to their championship puzzle and is willing to offer a nice package, a trade might make the most sense.

Six Gun Jury, @SixGunJury: Why is this manager resting everyone all the time during a 60-game season. If Burdi can’t pitch back to back nights, shouldn’t he be on the DL? lol

Starkey: I was initially surprised to not see Burdi for the ninth inning Monday night (Tuesday morning), Mr. Six Gun, but I completely understand not using him back-to-back days, given the severity of the injury that cost him last season. He better be the closer Tuesday, however.

Can you believe we are four games into the season and outraged at not seeing Nick Burdi?

I’ll tell you what: His stuff was electric against the Cardinals. I went back and watched that this morning, what he did to three very good hitters in Goldschmidt, DeJong and Carpenter. Wow.

Will it last? I’d like to find out.

Saint Patrick, @Pat5Slabe: Why do Feliz and Crick keep getting called upon? They do nothing but walk people and give up runs, they suck!

Starkey: Actually, Patrick — do you mind if I drop the “Saint?” — Feliz was excellent a day earlier in St. Louis. The problem was not having Crick up earlier, not that he’ll remind anyone of Goose Gossage at the moment. Thing is, when you have no closer, you need to have two relievers ready in case one implodes.

One did. It was odd seeing Feliz dig himself into a hole that grew deeper and deeper with nobody even warming up. Not sure what Shelton was thinking there, other than perhaps, “Who else do I have?”

John Antonik, @JohnAntonik: How about taking a crack at explaining the four-outfielder thing to me in the ninth with a four-run lead?

Starkey: I kind of liked it, John. One of my feelings going into this season was that I wanted to see the PIrates do some radical things. That was radical (although Justin Smoak wound up grounding a single through the right side). Also, it’s something the Tampa Bay Rays did more than any team in baseball last season (48 times), and anything the Rays are doing is something to consider. They’re ahead of the curve in many ways.

Shelton said this to Rob Biertempfel of The Athletic before the season, in regard to the possibility of using four outfielders: “The benefit is definitely with guys who don’t hit ground balls and who strike out, you’re covering more ground. We’re not going to do it just to do it. It’s not going to be something we’re doing as a sideshow. It’s going to depend on the pitcher, it’s going to depend on the guy swinging the bat, it’s going to depend on where we are in the game. There are a lot of factors that play into it.”
