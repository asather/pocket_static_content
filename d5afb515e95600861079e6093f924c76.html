Paul Zeise: NBA's G-League finally provides viable alternative to college

The G-League, which is the official minor league of the NBA, signed the No., 1 high school player in the country this week, Jalen Green of California. Isaiah Todd, considered one of the top-15 players in the country, followed suit and signed a contract, and soon there will be several others.

Apparently the G-League is offering packages that give these players the ability to make up to $500,000 through endorsements and salary. There is also a plan in place for them to have an ability to make even more money. They are guaranteed a college scholarship and will get one-on-one training for a year. It is all detailed in a Sports Illustrated column that claims the NBA and NCAA are now competitors.

There are some people who think this is a bad development. I can’t quite figure this out to be honest as

This may be the best news I’ve read in a long time regarding college basketball. Sure, it was fun watching the Zion Williamsons of the world come to college — many against their will — and play for a season, but it isn’t what’s best for the sport. Nor is it morally the right thing to do.

I’ve often said the NBA’s age limit rule (aka the one-and-done rule) goes against everything we stand for in a free country. If a high school player thinks he is ready to go to the NBA and wants to give it a try, he should be allowed to. The fact that these players have had to go through some sort of charade of going to school for a year before they are eligible to be drafted was a crime.

And please, spare me the silly nonsense of “Nobody forced them to go to college” as if playing in Europe is a legitimate option for most of these players. It’s silly to ask kids to leave their families and go to a foreign country to play in a professional league against grown men. If they can do that there, why can’t they just do it here in the United States in the NBA?

The flip side is the NBA put the rule in for one reason — so it can essentially keep a free minor league system. The NBA was tired of developing first round picks coming straight out of high school so it instituted a rule so that colleges could take on the expense of developing players.

It was as corrupt of a system as there was. The NBA is worth billions and shouldn’t be leaning on colleges to develop its players. And colleges shouldn’t be a place where players who have no interest in being in school are there because they want to go play basketball for a living.

Now we have the G-League, which is run and funded by the NBA, finally making it right for all involved — the players, the NBA and college basketball. Players, at least the top ones, have a chance to go straight into professional basketball and cash in immediately on their talent. The NBA gets a chance to develop players as well as find out about their talent at a deeply discounted rate over having to pay them as lottery picks.

And college basketball programs and coaches can try and build programs with players who, at least on some level, actually want to be there. This also could perhaps lessen — it won’t eliminate — the stench of payoffs and corruption that plagues the sport and has it currently mired in an FBI Investigation. Corrupt programs won’t be able to outbid the G-League for the top players they want, and that is a good thing.

This will also be great for players because they will develop more given the “Academy-like” instruction for a year. A college player is allowed an extremely limited number of hours of instruction over the summer. Then during the season there is about 20 hours a week they are allowed to be develop with coaches, not to mention classes, study halls and other obligations. A professional basketball player works on one thing — his basketball game. He can workout every day, he can practice as much as he needs and he will have the benefit of the best instruction in the world.

This isn’t a perfect solution, but it’s far superior to the idea of forcing one-and-done players to go to school for a year.
