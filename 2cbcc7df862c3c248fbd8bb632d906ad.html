Proving ground: Finish hyperloop tech before promising its impact

Few projects excite the public as much as the hyperloop, and with good reason. Promises of high speeds and low costs could mean a revolution in transportation, an area in sore need of modernization in the U.S. But that optimism must be tempered — at least temporarily — until it is proven that the technology can work as promised.

The potential of a hyperloop system, according to studies by groups like the Mid-Ohio Regional Planning Commission and the Northeast Ohio Areawide Coordinating Agency, is remarkable.

The hyper-fast pneumatic tube technology could feasibly carry passengers from Pittsburgh to Columbus, Ohio, in about 20 minutes for $33, and from Pittsburgh to Chicago in about 56 minutes for just $93. It has been estimated that the Pittsburgh-Columbus-Chicago route could create more than 900,000 jobs and $47.6 billion in increased income.

These are staggering numbers, indicative of the excitement and potential of a hyperloop system. But there are still significant logistical and technical hurdles that must be met before those numbers can become a reality.

There are legal and regulatory issues that must be solved, such as securing rights-of-way. But the more pressing concerns are technical. Hyperloop is still an unproven technology that has never been tested in real-world conditions. It is unclear if human passengers could even use the system due to the extreme speeds and G-force that would be exerted on them. And, if construction costs run higher than expected, as many economists and transportation experts expect, the promise of affordable travel could dissipate.

Hyperloop may still revolutionize transportation as we know it. The promise of day-trips between Pittsburgh and Chicago for under $100 is remarkable, and the potential for an economic boon is enormous. It is deserving of research, investment and excitement.

But before cities, regions and entire states bank on hyperloop, the technology must prove itself.
