Degrading impeachment

With the Senate’s acquittal of President Donald Trump, the impeachment melodrama finally ends.

Impeachment always seemed reckless — based as it was on vague charges and a rushed and incomplete investigation by the House and held, as it was, in the shadow of a new presidential election.

The Senate did no better. It held a trial without witnesses.

And the whole thing was brought on by the president’s own stupendous recklessness and hubris.

There were no good guys in this whole messy and sad affair.

And everyone knew the bottom line and ultimate outcome. Everyone knew the votes for impeachment would never be there. So what was the point?

The point was to assert once again that Mr. Trump should not be president — that he is “unworthy.”

But that is a word we should all be careful with. Barack Obama has supposedly said that Bernie Sanders is unworthy to be president. Some thought Mr. Obama was unworthy.

In the United States of America, anyone duly elected is worthy.

When the Watergate crisis happened, President Gerald Ford called it “our long national nightmare.” And it was. But there were good guys, and we all watched and learned and felt proud. The result was idealism.

In this case, the result is cynicism and an increased likelihood that the next Democratic president with a Republican House will be impeached.

Impeachment has been degraded, if not trivialized, and the Senate, House and presidency have been wounded and diminished.

We need to stop this. We need to end the politics of enmity and payback before we destroy ourselves.
