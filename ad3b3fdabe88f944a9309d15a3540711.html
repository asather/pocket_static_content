VoteSafe Pennsylvania begins effort to educate voters on how safely cast votes in November

A few dozen of Pennsylvania’s elected officials and party stalwarts — including former Pittsburgh Mayor Tom Murphy and all six of the state’s living former governors — will spend the next three-and-a-half months trying to educate voters on how they can cast their ballots in November’s election.

VoteSafe Pennsylvania, a coalition that launched a statewide campaign on Tuesday, will bring together officials from both parties to try to allay any fears voters may have about voting in the midst of a pandemic. They will try to explain to them their choices on how to safely do so — whether it be vote-by-mail or heading to an in-person precinct.

In addition to running an educational campaign, the coalition will advocate for counties to obtain more resources from the federal government — in order to ensure that voting by mail and in-person are safe, secure and accessible and that the results are counted in a timely manner, said co-chairman Dave Reed, former Republican Majority Leader of the state House.

“No matter the outcome [of November’s election], ensuring that everybody has the opportunity to have their vote be counted and are confident that they’re counted accurately, I think, is going to be essential to the country and our state moving forward, whatever direction it may be,” Mr. Reed said.

Signing onto the initiative are former Govs. Tom Corbett, Ed Rendell, Mark Schweiker, Tom Ridge and Dick Thornburgh, as well as former Pennsylvania House Speaker Dennis O’Brien. Co-chairing the committee alongside Mr. Reed is former U.S. Rep. Patrick Murphy, a Democrat who served as Undersecretary of the Army.

The coalition is operating under the assumption that November’s election won’t be typical and that there will still be lingering effects of the COVID-19 pandemic, Mr. Reed said.

But it also will operate at a time of stark partisan divide over the validity of vote-by-mail, amplified by President Donald Trump.

Mr. Trump tweeted Tuesday morning that “Mail-In Voting, unless changed by the courts, will lead to the most CORRUPT ELECTION in our Nation’s History! #RIGGEDELECTION.”

Asked about the president’s tweet — which came on the same day of the coalition’s launch — Mr. Reed said the coalition will “let presidential politics play out” and that “things are going to be said” by both parties and candidates on the issue.

“From our perspective, we’re focused on making sure everyone feels comfortable with the opportunity to vote this fall,” Mr. Reed said. “It’s really that simple.”

But does the president’s rhetoric complicate their efforts to educate voters?

It’s “sometimes more difficult,” Mr. Reed said, “when elected officials attack different items” — but it can also have the effect of drawing more attention to the option of mail-in balloting, which the coalition aims to do on its own.

A press release announcing VoteSafe’s statewide campaign included the assertion, “Despite the many myths about voting by mail, the reality is that mail-in ballots are a safe and secure method, and do not advantage one party over the other.”

Mr. Reed said he doesn’t believe mail-in ballots benefit one party over another, citing the success of the absentee ballot system in Pennsylvania over many years. 

The success of this coalition, Mr. Reed said, will be measured by how confident voters are in the voting process and how timely election results are reported.
