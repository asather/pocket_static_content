Gene Therapy: Trump did nothing wrong? 'Ok, I'm out'

Some days, you should just listen to yourself, you know? 

Some days, your impulses might just be right.

Some days, that first burst of snark you throw out on Twitter should enjoy some actual shelf life and even provide a blueprint for the rest of the day. Or the week.

So last Saturday, after hearing Mr. Trump’s lawyer Pat Cipollone open the defense case in the Impeachment trial by telling me that when I hear the facts I will find “the president did absolutely nothing wrong,” I tweeted “Ok, I’m out.”

Admittedly, I didn’t even have time to form a thought, but I’m pretty sure the thought would have been something like, “Wait so, Paul Manafort, Rick Gates, George Papadopoulos and Michael Cohen went to jail, Roger Stone was convicted, Michael Flynn pleaded guilty, Rudy Giuliani is under investigation, and 28 others including 12 Russian nationals were indicted through the Mueller investigation, but the president, Donald John Trump, for whom all of their varying degrees of skullduggery were derived to assist, “did absolutely nothing wrong.”

Would that I’d stuck with “Ok I’m out.”

But no, I had to go back in and watch six more days of chain-reaction rhetorical wreckage on the bridge Mr. Trump’s illogical jurisprudence engineers tried to build between the president and innocence.

So I got to see Pam Bondi, erstwhile Florida Attorney General, posit that Mr. Trump’s main interest in Ukraine was not excavating dirt on his most-feared political opponent, Joe Biden, it was because Mr. Trump cares so much about fighting corruption.

Pam. Bondi.

The same person who was poised to join the investigation of Trump University until a $25,000 check from  Mr. Trump showed up her re-election fund. Trump U, the Fighting Fraudsters — wound up costing the Donald $25 million in fines, but it was worth it because now Pam fully understands his passion for corruption. I mean for fighting corruption. No, I think I mean for corruption.

Pam was hysterical, side-splitting, but it was time for your headliner. Ladies and gentleman, The Man Who Used To Be Alan Dershowitz.

Let me see if I can just crystallize for you what the esteemed Harvard professor said over the course of four days, the worst of it Wednesday night: Trump is king, and you are not. If it pleaseth the king, the king shall doeth it with impunity.

Millions went to their windows, just to see if they were still in America.

“Every public official that I know believes that his election is in the public interest,” Mr. Dershowitz said. “And if a president does something which he believes will help him get elected in the public interest, that cannot be the kind of quid pro quo that results in impeachment.”

Mr. Dershowitz then spent much of Thursday trying to walk that back, as they love to say, although it was a kind of slapstick moonwalk that accused everyone either of willful distortion or conscientious stupidity, including just about every legal scholar worth his or her summa cum laude. Mr. Dershowitz can only hope what he said was willfully distorted because, otherwise, the very nature of the presidency and the very nature of the country are changed forever.

Yeah, that’s all.

Mr. Dershowitz’s pre-trial comments on what it takes to remove a president in 2020 were exactly 180 degrees from he said about the same prerequisites in 1998, but when called on it, he said he was correct then but that he is more correct now. This decades-long march toward ultracorrectness appears to be leading to infallibility, much as in the case of His Royal Trumpness. But it has infected Mr. Dershowitz’s memory.

Less than four years ago, author Alan Dershowitz (then somewhere between correct and more correct) wrote a book called “Electile Dysfunction, a Guide for Unaroused Voters,” in which he noted that Mr. Trump panders “to hard-right bigots who hate minorities, immigrants, Muslims, Jews, and the ‘establishment,’” and he goes on to recommend that voters choose Hillary Clinton in part because “based on their history and track records, I trust Trump less than I do Clinton. On the issue of tone, there is little doubt Trump has been extraordinarily negative, mocking the disabled, referring to a woman’s menstrual cycle and a man’s penis, using racist, sexist, and scatological language — and in general being crass and bombastic. For voters who favor such “direct” talk, the choice of Trump may be suitable. For those of us who seek a president from whom our children and grandchildren can learn good manners and basic decency, and who is more presidential in tone, the choice of Clinton should seem obvious. On the overall question of who would make a better — and certainly a less dangerous — president, I have little doubt. That’s why I will vote for Clinton.”

But here, at one of the most critical points in American history, this same legal scholar is twisting himself into a perfect fool to effectively extend his own dark prophecy.

This “trial” may be all over but the gloating, but the fallout has only just begun.
