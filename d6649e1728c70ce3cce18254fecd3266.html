Mark Rylance's 'Steel,' about the Battle of Homestead, is now a commissioned six-part cycle

Oscar- and Tony-winning actor Mark Rylance's long-gestating theater project about the Battle of Homestead has found two homes outside of Pittsburgh.

“Steel,” by Rylance and Peter Reder, has been commissioned by the Guthrie Theatre of Minneapolis and American Repertory Theater at Harvard University in Cambridge, Mass.

In 2017, Rylance came to Pittsburgh to help commemorate the 125th anniversary of the 1892 Battle of Homestead, the strike by locked-out steelworkers that culminated in a violent clash with Pinkerton guards hired Andrew Carnegie and Henry Clay Frick.

Rylance, the first artistic director of Shakespeare's Globe Theatre and winner of a supporting actor Oscar for “Bridge of Spies,” came to Munhall and was joined by local actors in performing Shakespeare monologues and texts and songs that marked the Homestead anniversary.

Researching “Steel” brought Rylance to Pittsburgh on and off for more than a decade, and now will be a six-part American history cycle, directed by Rylance’s wife, Claire van Kampen.

The online story by American Theatre Magazine Friday that announced the co-commission quoted Rylance as he spoke at the Carnegie Library of Homestead during a reading of his work. He said the history of Pittsburgh steel “is a story worthy of Shakespeare. We should never forget the role of labor and industry and what it’s done for a nation. This story is essential to American culture.”

Rylance’s connection with Pittsburgh goes back to his “Hamlet” at the Pittsburgh Public Theater in 1991 — a production shared only with A.R.T. in Cambridge. He brought his Globe Theatre company here twice, in 2003 playing Olivia in “Twelfth Night” and in 2005, playing the Duke in “Measure for Measure.”
