Heinz Endowments boosts COVID-19 relief efforts by $6 million

The Heinz Endowments is doling out another $6 million in grants targeted for COVID-19 relief efforts in the Pittsburgh region.

The funding includes money to be used for public and environmental health initiatives, food, small business assistance, and support for artists and arts groups.

The endowments, a Downtown-based philanthropy created from the fortunes of heirs to the H.J. Heinz food conglomerate, last month formed a $5-million emergency fund to address critical needs during the pandemic and made 43 grants to nonprofits involved in relief.

At a board meeting last week, its directors unanimously approved supplementing those efforts with $6.4 million.

“A crisis like this tests nothing so much as what we truly value,” Grant Oliphant, president of the endowments said in a statement. “These grants express a commitment to a community that prizes its creativity, its environment, its potential, and the need shared by all its people for everything from food and shelter to dignity and meaning.”

The new funding includes 18 grants totaling $4.5 million plus nearly $2 million in a contingency fund for artists, arts groups, and relief and outreach efforts in the Hazelwood neighborhood.

The endowments is a partner in Hazelwood Green, a development located on a former steel mill site along the Monongahela River in Hazelwood, and has been working for years to integrate the development with the adjacent community.

The largest grant in the endowments’ new funding round is $775,000 to the Southwest Pennsylvania Environmental Health Project, which will help craft policies on public health impacts of the coronavirus.

Other large grants include $600,000 to Trying Together, an advocacy agency for childcare and education; $400,000 to Locally Grown to support entrepreneurs and residents at its business accelerator and community center in Wilkinsburg; $200,000 to Bridgeway Capital to assist small businesses impacted by the pandemic; and $230,000 to the Hill Community Development Corp. for programs that address health and economic impacts of the virus in the Hill District.

At last week’s board meeting, the endowments also approved $7 million in grants focused on equity issues including criminal justice reform and support for minorities in the region.

Though that funding is separate from its COVID-19 relief, Mr. Oliphant acknowledged the pandemic is “exacerbating issues of poverty and racial inequity in our community.”
