Pitt partners with 3D printing company to develop reusable N95 mask filters

The much-touted N-95 mask is reportedly effective at preventing the transmission of COVID-19, but new mask technology just over the horizon.

A partnership between the University of Pittsburgh and North Huntingdon-based industrial 3D printing company ExOne has yielded new metal filters currently in testing for filtration and airflow with the goal of competing with the N95â€™s standard. (The N95 is so named as it filters at least 95% of airborne particles.)

The new metal filters would could be sterilized for repeated reuse, which could help with the current shortage of personal protective equipment and reduce medical waste.

Currently, an issue with the N95 mask is that it can only be sterilized a few times before losing its integrity.

ExOne is using copper, which is known to have antibacterial properties, and stainless steel and testing a wide range of porosity levels. If the design is successful, the filters can be 3D printed in a variety of shapes to fit respirators, ventilators, anesthesia masks and other equipment.
