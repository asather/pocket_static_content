Sides make closing arguments in the Wilkinsburg mass shooting trial

Both sides in the Wilkinsburg mass murder trial have made their closing arguments and the jury will get the case later Monday afternoon following a lunch break and the judge's instructions.

They will consider both first- and third-degree murder charges against Cheron Shelton, who is accused of killing five adults and an unborn child on March 9, 2016 in Wilkinsburg.

Prosecutors believe Shelton, 33, of Lincoln-Lemington and another man, Robert Thomas, who was charged but had his case dismissed one week ago for lack of evidence, opened fire on a group of unsuspecting victims during a backyard cookout, spurred by a vendetta Shelton had against one of the attendees.

Shelton's lawyer, Randall McKinney, spent an hour trying to convince jurors that the prosecution's case amounted to a series of red herrings and weak evidence that investigators tried to bend to their will in order to implicate Shelton.

Mr. McKinney dissected each piece of the prosecution's evidence — surveillance video, a cache of ammunition found in Shelton's mother's house, letters sent from jail and jailhouse video — and tried to convince jurors to view it in a light favorable to Shelton. While prosecutors strung the evidence together and said it pointed to Shelton being guilty, Mr. McKinney asked the jury to attribute far more benign motives.

Mr. McKinney cast his client as a drug dealer seeking to make money, one who had just been released from jail the day before the shooting over a domestic dispute and was happy and eager to see his girlfriend, their children and his friends.

Shelton, he said, is not a ruthless killer who set out to gun down members of a family as an act of revenge.

Mr. McKinney told jurors that prosecutors must take a brick wall that represented proof beyond a reasonable doubt and entirely dismantle the obstacle so that not a single brick remains. He contended that the Allegheny County District Attorney's Office had not done that.

He acknowledged a natural urge to hold someone accountable for horrific crimes.

"But you have to resist it," Mr. McKinney said, "because the evidence in this case, the lack of evidence in this case, proves to us that the only appropriate verdict is not guilty."

Deputy District Attorney Kevin Chernosky, who had the last words in the courtroom in asking the jury for a guilty verdict, dismissed Mr. McKinney's arguments.

He acknowledged that prosecutors had a circumstantial case — no fingerprints, DNA, eyewitnesses or murder weapons — but he said they had a timeline and surveillance video that taken together put Shelton leaving his mother's house in Homewood with a rifle, arriving at the crime scene and then leaving after the shooting at 10:53 p.m.

"This case is a circumstantial case, and sometimes that comes off with a bad connotation. But the circumstances don't lie to you," Mr. Chernosky said.

Holding up a long, battered piece of painted blue wood from the crime scene, Mr. Chernosky said, "This railing was the only thing between him and the victims in this case when he opened fire unmercifully."

There were 48 shots total fired — 18 from a .40-caliber handgun that police believe Thomas used, and 30 from a rifle firing 7.62-caliber bullets that they say Shelton wielded.

"There were times when he stopped," Mr. Chernosky said, alluding to the sound of gunfire that was played for the jury earlier in the trial, "there were times when he could have stopped."
