Inspired by Bethel Park family, Congress moves to shed light on unexplained deaths in children

WASHINGTON — More than three years ago, Stephanie Zarecky of Bethel Park faced every parent's worst nightmare: The death of her daughter, Scarlett Lillian Pauley, at 16 months old.

The Allegheny County Medical Examiner could not determine the cause of Scarlett's death, placing her in a confounding category of death called Sudden Unexplained Death in Childhood, or SUDC. Ms. Zarecky, while still grappling at this mystery, turned to advocacy to raise awareness of the issue. 

Now, new legislation moving through Congress would require more research into such childhood deaths and standardize death investigations to glean more information. The bill — introduced by Sen. Bob Casey, D-Pa., and supported by Rep. Mike Doyle, D-Forest Hills — carries her daughter’s name: Scarlett’s Sunshine on Unexpected Death Act.

“We’ve done far too little for far too long to really move the needle on what’s happening with these kids,” Ms. Zarecky said in an interview Monday. “We can’t just accept that sometimes healthy kids just die. We can’t accept that notion.

“I don’t know why this happened to Scarlett, so I don’t know why it won’t happen to my other daughter or other children I may have someday,” she said. “And I don’t know why it won’t happen to my family or my friends and their kids.”

SUDC is defined as a sudden or unexpected death in children who are at least 1 year of age that remains unexplained after a thorough investigation, including an autopsy. These unexplained deaths occur in about 400 American children each year, according to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, making it the fifth leading category of death in young children.

An additional 3,600 infants are lost each year to Sudden Unexpected Infant Death, or SUID, which is the sudden death of an infant under 1 year of age that, when first discovered, did not have an obvious cause.

Because of the lack of a standardized way to attribute deaths related to those two unexplained categories, it is impossible to know how widespread the problem is, said Ms. Zarecky, who is currently the ambassador program and public relations manager for the SUDC Foundation, a New Jersey-based nonprofit.

“We need good data,” Ms. Zarecky said, “that will hopefully eventually lead us to a point where we can have more research and figure out why these deaths are happening and figure out how to prevent them in the future.”

Last April, Mr. Casey introduced legislation aimed at strengthening efforts to better understand the deaths by encouraging data collection and analysis to improve prevention efforts.

The bill, among other things, directs the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services to assess whether a death investigation reporting form needs to be updated. And it requires the department to submit annual reports to Congress including a count of deaths and actions taken to prevent them. 

The bill passed the Senate unanimously in May. Last week, the House Energy and Commerce Committee unanimously advanced the bill to the House floor.

In January, three years to the day since she lost her daughter, Ms. Zarecky testified before the health care panel of the House Energy and Commerce Committee. She recounted for lawmakers the memories of Scarlett’s death: “Three years and 10 hours ago, a doctor told us there was nothing more they could do and my Scarlett, the very reason for every beat of my heart and every breath I took, was gone.”

The autopsy results, after five months of waiting, revealed no cause. Ms. Zarecky then went to New York University to seek another review of Scarlett's death. That, too, turned up no answers.

She said she felt honored the bill was named in Scarlett’s honor. Any parent who has lost a child, she said, fears their child’s memory will be forgotten. 

“I feel like as long as I can keep speaking her name and keep sharing her story, that in some way it keeps her alive,” she said. “So we really need to keep pushing.”
