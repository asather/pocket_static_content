Casey, 32 other senators call for $300 million in domestic violence funding

WASHINGTON — Sen. Bob Casey, D-Pa., is demanding another $300 million for domestic violence programs, saying the National Domestic Violence Hotline has reported abusers taking advantage of the social distancing guidelines to isolate victims from their friends and family.

Mr. Casey, joined by Sen. Amy Klobuchar, D-Minn., and Sen. Lisa Murkowski, R-Alaska, led a letter to Senate leadership and appropriators, urging them to allocate the funding in the next round of COVID-19 legislation. The letter, signed by a total of 33 senators from both parties, stated that some abusers are believed to be withholding financial resources or even medical aid.

The call for more funding comes amid abuse worries as families and children are cooped up at home, per government health guidelines. About 54 million K-12 students are no longer going to school and high unemployment has ravaged the country, the letter stated.

“Simply put, even though staying home is currently our best way to slow the spread of this deadly virus, home is not a safe place for people who experience domestic violence,” the senators wrote. “As a result, communities around the country are reporting increased demands on victim service providers, which are facing new difficulties and complexities in assisting survivors.”

The senators called for funding for shelters, resource centers and technical assistance to support victims of domestic violence, including an additional $100 million for programs funded by the Family Violence Prevention and Services Act, the primary federal funding source for domestic violence services like Domestic Violence Hotline.

The senators asked for another at least $100 million for the Centers of Disease Control and Prevention to coordinate language-accessible public outreach to hard-to-reach populations. They requested $101 million be set aside for tribes.

Domestic violence advocates praised the letter. 

“News reports from across the nation and across the globe have documented a significant increase in domestic violence incidences,” said Ruth M. Glenn, president and CEO of the National Coalition Against Domestic Violence. “Our member programs are telling us that they do not have the resources to serve all of the survivors seeking help.”

Despite the wide support demonstrated in Mr. Casey’s letter, the Republican-controlled Senate has remained gridlocked on upcoming bills. Republicans and Democrats blocked each other’s proposals to replenish a small business loan program last Thursday. 

Congress’ first COVID-19 relief bill, signed into law in early March, lawmakers included $45 million to provide more support to family violence shelters and $2 million for the National Domestic Violence Hotline.

Pittsburgh domestic violence programs reported last week a decline in calls. Advocates told the Post-Gazette they believe victims have less time alone to make calls for help or have the incorrect perception that the pandemic has closed the shelters.
