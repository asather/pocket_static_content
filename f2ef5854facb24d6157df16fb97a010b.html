Tom Savini helps create masks that are scary and protective

Some people have taken to baking artisan bread or taking long walks during the pandemic.

Tom Savini is doing what he knows and loves — creating scary but really useful masks.

“They are safety masks, they are PPE and they have been selling like crazy,” said Mr. Savini, a Bloomfield native who is legendary for his special makeup effects.

Jason Baker and a group of 10 others have producing creepy resin masks with filters, based on Savini’s sculpt, at a studio near Bakery Square. Designs include “Friday the 13th” Jason hockey masks, zombie heads and skulls.

Baker is a graduate of the Tom Savini Special Make-up Effects program at the Douglas Education Center in Monessen, as well as the George A. Romero film school there. He had been working as Mr. Savini’s assistant.

“It’s become an industry,” Mr. Savini said. “We thought we were going to sell maybe four or five of these to nerds like ourselves.”

The masks — which might soon include a likeness from the William Castle horror film “Mr. Sardonicus” — go for $60, $100 if autographed. Baker’s social media accounts (@bakingjason) direct interested parties to email requests to callowsumfxstudios@gmail.com.

Since they’re made from a hard plastic, they can be scrubbed. “You can even use Windex on it,” said Mr. Savini, noting that his grandson, James, is helping out.

He said they are sensitive to the realities of the pandemic.

“I have to say that after the virus broke out and I saw all these commercials for face masks and gloves, I thought ‘Wow, look at all these people capitalizing on this disaster.’

“But now we look at where we are at. These are ‘monsters’ for the nerd populace that loves this stuff.’”

That includes the masks’ designer. He shared a photo of one with a lurid purple, melting face.

“I’ve always wanted a Vincent Price bust, with his makeup from ‘House of Wax.’” 

Mr. Savini is also a writer, director and actor. He appeared as “Old Snake” in the most recent episode of AMC’s “NOS4A2,” and recently made cameos in Netflix’s “Locke & Key.” Members of the series’ high school horror film club refer to themselves as “Savinis.”

Closer to home, Mr. Savini can be spotted every day in Lawrenceville, where artist Jeremy Raymer created a mural of him on the side of a house on Almond Way.

He had planned to appear at a big convention in Texas in December, “although Texas is looking pretty bad.” When conventions resume, he plans to  bring along a selection of his masks. That includes his custom favorite, a black version of Jason.

“I’ll sit there with a mask on every day, maybe wear two different ones.” 

For now, the idea of attending any large gathering is its own horror show, he said. “But it’s like my wife says about a lot of things: ‘This is temporary. It’s not going to last forever.’”
