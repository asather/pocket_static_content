NYSE warns GNC its shares could be delisted

New York Stock Exchange officials have warned GNC it is in danger of being delisted, a move that would strike another serious financial blow to the Pittsburgh-based health and wellness retailer.

The warning, dated April 22 and disclosed by the company on Tuesday, is based on two factors: GNC shares averaging below $1 at closing and its average market capitalization falling below $50 million over 30 trading days.

As of April 21, GNC’s average closing share price was 56 cents and its market capitalization was $47.3 million.

GNC has six months to become compliant but, in a release Tuesday, the company said, “No assurance can be given that the company will be able to regain compliance.”

The company also acknowledged the serious financial implications if its shares are suspended or delisted.

Should that happen, the GNC release said, “It would likely reduce the liquidity and market price of the shares; reduce the number of investors willing to hold or acquire the shares; negatively impact the company’s ability to access equity markets and obtain financing; and impair the company’s ability to attract and retain employees by means of equity compensation.”

GNC, whose shares were trading near $50 five years ago, saw its stock price fall precipitously in the 2015-2017 period, starting a downward trend that has slowed but never quite reversed.

The company did make notable moves to right its financial ship, rebranding itself as One New GNC in December 2016 and, after changing CEOs three times between 2014-2017, bringing stability to the c-suite with the appointment of current President and CEO Ken Martindale in September 2017.

Under Mr. Martindale’s leadership, GNC has expanded its global reach, partnering with Harbin Pharmaceutical Group which included a $300 million investment by the Chinese company, signing a master franchise agreement with Rapid Nutrition in Australia and, most recently, reaching a partnership with Brazilian retailer BFG Brasil Comercial de Vitaminas LTDA.

But online sales of vitamins and supplements by competitors, an excess of brick-and-mortar stores and mounting debt, have continued to weigh down the company.

Earlier this month, GNC announced it was temporarily furloughing “a significant portion” of its employees after negotiations to refinance some $600 million in debt fell through.
