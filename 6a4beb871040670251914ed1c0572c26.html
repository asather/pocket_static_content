Late billionaire Scaife's son, grandson propose $200M settlement of trust fund battle

Two of late billionaire publisher Richard Mellon Scaife’s heirs have proposed a $200 million settlement to resolve a long-running, high-stakes court battle over a drained trust fund.

But the suggested settlement represents less than half the amount at issue in more than five years of running litigation over whether the fund was improperly drained of roughly $450 million.

And the suggested settlement — pitched by Mr. Scaife’s son, grandson and two of the fund’s three trustees — leaves out one key player: the estate of his late daughter.

“We were not a part of this process, and we might have a lot to say, but I need to evaluate all this and engage with my client,” lawyer William Pietragallo II, who represents the estate of Jennie Scaife, said Wednesday.

Ms. Scaife died childless in 2018. David Zywiec, the executor of Ms. Scaife's estate, is president of the Scaife Family Foundation in West Palm Beach, Fla. The foundation is the principal beneficiary of her estate.

The petition was filed Tuesday in Allegheny County’s Orphans’ Court division.

Mr. Scaife, who owned the Tribune-Review media properties, died in 2014. His children — David N. Scaife and Jennie Scaife — were supposed to get whatever was left in a trust fund bequeathed by his mother.

But the children found that the principal in the once-gargantuan nest egg, referred to as the 1935 Trust, was gone.

The children claimed that the fund’s trustees — PNC Bank, James M. Walton and H. Yale Gutnick — had improperly handled the money. They accused the trustees of emptying the fund of $450 million in principal over a 20-year period in order to allow Mr. Scaife to prop up the Tribune-Review.

The son and daughter sought to show that the trustees wasted the fund’s assets, at least in part because Mr. Scaife wanted to “disinherit his children,” as a state Superior Court judge put it in a recent decision.

E.J. Strassburger, who represents Mr. Gutnick, declined comment, as did Thomas Frampton, Mr. Walton's lawyer. Other attorneys involved in the case could not be reached for comment.

In the petition, David N. Scaife, his son, David G. Scaife, Mr. Walton and PNC Bank have come together to ask the court to endorse their suggested settlement plan.

The petitioners signed off on the settlement agreement on Dec. 30, and Mr. Gutnick earlier this month agreed to be bound by certain parts of it.

Now a judge has to approve it.

Of the $200 million, which would come from Mr. Scaife’s estate, $24.3 million would be earmarked for his son and his daughter’s estate, the petition says.

That amount represents income that would have been generated between July 2014, when Mr. Scaife died, and November 2018, when his daughter died, had the fund still had $200 million in principal.

The rest, according to the settlement terms, would be returned to the 1935 Trust.

While Mr. Gutnick is not part of the joint petition, it says that he does not object to the settlement.

The agreement calls for PNC Bank, Mr. Walton and Mr. Gutnick to resign as trustees and be replaced by trustees suggested by Mr. Scaife’s son and grandson.
