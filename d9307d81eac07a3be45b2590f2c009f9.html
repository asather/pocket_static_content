State Police prize education, encouragement over enforcement actions for COVID-19 violations

If that rural hair salon, suburban mom-and-pop shop or urban bar opens in violation of the governor’s orders, don’t expect state troopers to swoop in immediately, sirens blaring, and start hauling proprietors away.

Instead, “willful noncompliance” will continue to be the trigger for aggressive enforcement of pandemic orders by Pennsylvania State Police as counties transition from red to yellow status during the COVID-19 crisis, a top agency official said Wednesday.

Short of that, the commonwealth’s 4,500 state troopers will continue to work on education and encouragement as a first step to deal with violations of Gov. Tom Wolf’s various orders to combat the spread of the novel coronavirus.

So far, Lt. Col. Scott Price noted during a conference call with media from across the state, enforcement actions have been limited since March. Across the state, troopers have issued 329 warnings to business but only one citation; and 34 warnings and seven citations for violations of the governor’s stay-at-home order.

One such incident was an underage drinking party at an Econo Lodge motel room in Snyder County, where three people from other counties gathered in violation of the stay-at-home order. That, Lt. Col. Price said, would count as willful noncompliance, as would having to respond multiple times to a call or someone having multiple violations.

Troopers have mostly dealt with a cooperative populace and misunderstandings about the governor’s orders, he said.

Often those have been cleared up with conversation rather than confrontation. And while Lt. Price hopes that trend will continue, he said troopers will invariably become more proactive — and low crime rates during the pandemic might start to rebound — as counties move from red to yellow status as a result of more traffic, more people out and about and more businesses open.

Conflict is likely inevitable, though. Lt. Col. Price said in areas where the state police are not the primary law enforcement agency and serve as backup to the locals, “Our posture pretty much remains the same...We don’t intercede or second-guess the actions taken by municipal police departments. ... Are we going to go in and enforce the governor’s orders if a municipal police force doesn’t? And the answer to that is no, that’s not how we operate.”

In places where state police have primary jurisdiction, they will enforce as needed. And if the local district attorney opts not to prosecute, that’s not an unfamiliar situation. It happens already, Lt. Col. Price said, in arrests for small amounts of marijuana.

‘We will take the appropriate enforcement action based upon the law...and we respect the district attorney’s discretion to not prosecute beyond that.”

As for business that open unlawfully, state police plan to approach such situations with restraint — but from the position that county rules do not outrank state dictates.

“We would encourage compliance and likely engage in a conversation with the Department of Health, determine whether the Department of Health would want to pursue some sort of closure order for the business,” Lt. Col. Price said, answering a hypothetical question about a restaurant reopening without proper authorization. “I think we would maintain the position that the county doesn’t have the authority to supersede that order .”

State police are acutely aware of the rift between some counties and the governor’s office on plans to phase in reopening, and the agency is monitoring the situation.

Just this week, the governor rebuked counties and business that plan to reopen without state approval.

But for now, according to Lt. Col. Price, both sides have been “engaging in a discourse” that is part of democracy. Troopers have not been deployed out of concern that serious problems might arise. 

“We’re aware of the situation,” he said. “We’re aware that it’s sometimes contentious.”
