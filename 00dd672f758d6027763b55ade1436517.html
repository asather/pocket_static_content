Republicans likely have the votes to end Trump impeachment trial

WASHINGTON — With Sen. Lamar Alexander, R-Tenn., announcing his opposition late Thursday, the Senate is likely to have the votes to block witnesses in the impeachment trial of President Donald Trump.

All 100 senators will gather in the gilded chamber to vote whether to effectively end the impeachment trial or expand its scope in a potentially dramatic way. If the Senate rejects efforts, led by Democrats, to hear any more information, it could move to acquit Mr. Trump. 

Mr. Alexander, seen as the swing vote, said in a statement he believed Mr. Trump committed the acts House Democrats alleged. But he said he did not believe the president’s actions were impeachable.

“It was inappropriate for the president to ask a foreign leader to investigate his political opponent and to withhold United States aid to encourage that investigation,” Mr. Alexander wrote shortly after senators’ questioning wrapped up. “There is no need for more evidence to prove something that has already been proven.”

However, “the Constitution does not give the Senate the power to remove the president from office and ban him from this year’s ballot simply for actions that are inappropriate,” he stated.

The Republican-controlled Senate had been playing a political tug of war with four members in particular: Mr. Alexander, Sen. Susan Collins, R-Maine, Sen. Lisa Murkowski, R-Alaska, and Sen. Mitt Romney, R-Utah. 

Ms. Collins announced Thursday she would vote to call witnesses. Mr Romney is also likely in favor of witnesses.

Even if Ms. Murkowski would vote for witnesses, too, that would result in a 50-50 tie, assuming all 45 Democrats — and the two Independents who often join Democrats — vote in favor. 

Chief Justice John Roberts could intervene to break the tie, but it is unclear whether he would do so.

Ms. Murkowski, however, seemed skeptical in a late-hour question posed Thursday to White House lawyers. She, along with Mr. Alexander, joined a question that asked, even if Mr. Bolton were to testify, “isn’t it true that the allegations [against Mr. Trump] would not rise to an impeachable offense” and that Mr. Bolton’s testimony “would add nothing?”

If senators vote to block witnesses, Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer, D-N.Y., told reporters on Thursday he had tactics up his sleeve to delay a speedy acquittal. 

“The minority has rights, and we will exercise those rights,” Mr. Schumer told reporters. 

The Senate will convene at 1 p.m. today.
