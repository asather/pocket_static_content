Public hearings, meetings ahead for parks tax issue

At least one public hearing on how to handle the new parks tax revenue has been scheduled for early March.

Councilman Anthony Coghill confirmed Wednesday that a public hearing will take place on March 5 at 6 p.m. in Council Chambers to discuss a proposal that will create a trust fund for the new tax revenue.

The governing body agreed last week to start over on the enabling legislation to create a fund for the estimated $10 million a year expected to be generated for the city’s 165 parks.

The money will come from a half-mill property tax increase — $50 on every $100,000 of assessed real estate value — that voters narrowly approved in November after the nonprofit Pittsburgh Parks Conservancy, with the Mayor Bill Peduto’s support, petitioned for the ballot referendum.

An original iteration of the bill introduced by Mr. Coghill and Councilwoman Deb Gross that aimed to split the money equally among nine council districts was met with criticism from Councilman Ricky Burgess and the city’s Law Department. Now council is working with a shell of a bill until hearing from the public and debating the issue further.

A separate public hearing for the mayor’s bill on the official creation of the tax will also be scheduled, though a date has not yet been released. Mr. Burgess said council hopes to hold the hearings on the same day, though that has not been confirmed.

Mr. Burgess, who chairs the Urban Recreation Committee, introduced a third piece of legislation Tuesday, aiming to adopt the conservancy’s “Parks Plan” as the city’s official road map for using the parks revenue. 

On Wednesday he motioned to hold a special fact-finding meeting on the plan. He was met with requests from Ms. Gross, Mr. Coghill and Council President Theresa Kail-Smith to arrange for presentations on the conservancy’s finances and more detailed data sets that explain the conservancy’s rationale in creating the “Parks Plan.”

The conservancy’s multi-year plan proposes allocating money for routine maintenance, capital projects and programming according to an equity and needs-based formula.

“The kind of glossy brochure has been presented publicly, that is available on the [conservancy’s] website,” Ms. Gross said, adding that she wants to see “the raw data.”

Ms. Kail-Smith said her “ultimate goal is to find who funded and pushed the plan.”

According to Oct. 25 campaign finance reports, the parks conservancy contributed $634,000 in direct funding to the ballot referendum campaign, as well as $80,000 in in-kind contributions. The Pittsburgh Urban Magnet Project, or PUMP, directly contributed $45,000.

Mr. Burgess agreed to work with members but said that his concern is focused on how the new tax revenue will be spent.

“That’s my interest. My advocacy for the plan has nothing to do with any outside organization, lobbying or promotion. My intention is solely, narrowly focused on how the money is spent,” he said.

The conservancy did not immediately respond for comment.
