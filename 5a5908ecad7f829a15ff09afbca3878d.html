Derek Shelton remains confident in Pirates' COVID-19 response

CHICAGO — Even as the 2020 season appears to be on shaky ground, a conclusion drawn after some harsh words from MLB commissioner Rob Manfred delivered to union chief Tony Clark, manager Derek Shelton remains confident that the Pirates have done — and continue to do — all the right things.

A father of three, Shelton obviously worries about the coronavirus pandemic from a societal standpoint, but giving the Pirates manager comfort is the fact that they’ve seemingly grasped the severity of this from the beginning.

As an example, Shelton cited what the Pirates did on March 3, more than a week before the sport shut down. That’s when, “out of an overabundance of caution,” they conducted a “deep clean” of LECOM Park in Bradenton, Fla.

“Our guys are doing a heck of a job,” Shelton said before Friday’s game against the Cubs at Wrigley Field. “They’re following the protocols, and we’ve continued to stay on top of it. That’s a credit organizationally.”

The same likely cannot be said for all MLB teams, which is why this season could wind up being less than two weeks long.

According to ESPN, which broke the story of Manfred warning Clark that he may shut the season down if things don’t improve, a big point of contention has been players failing to follow the health and safety protocols.

Testing data released by MLB on Friday indicated that, in the past week, 29 positive tests were reported, including 21 from one team (the Miami Marlins). Also included in that total are two Cardinals players who got positive tests back late Thursday.

The fallout has forced eight teams to postpone games, and it apparently has Manfred and local officials concerned that players are not following the 113-page operations manual.

In Pittsburgh, Shelton does not see his players cutting corners or doing anything stupid, nor did he express serious concern that the season could conceivably be canceled by Monday.

“I think it’s good that they’re getting together and talking because, as we’ve talked about with everything, we’re continuing to adjust,” Shelton said. “We’ve had a consistent message with our group about trusting each other. Trust the people you’re around. Trust the decisions you’re making. That’s been our message. We’ve continued to hammer it.”

Not that it’s been without a few difficult conservations. While Shelton said he has not had any players who were seriously close to opting out, a few have come forward with concerns.

Those have been handled with open and honest discussions, Shelton said. And by listening.

“We’re going to educate,” Shelton said. “We’re going to be compassionate. We’re going to talk about it. And we want to hear voices. So if people have feelings, we are definitely addressing them.

“Normally they’re not addressed by me. They’re addressed by people who are a lot smarter than me and have a way better understanding of the medical and the protocols. We just make sure we get guys the right answers.”

‘Eye-opening experience’

From a player perspective, Mitch Keller said it was “eye-opening” to have the COVID-19 situation consume the Cardinals, a division rival. Before that, the virus was mostly affecting teams in the National League East and American League East.

To that end, the Pirates have begun wearing N95 masks when they travel, around the hotel and at the ballpark. That represents a step up from the cloth ones they had been wearing.

“We’ve been informed on how to prepare and keep ourselves as safe as possible," Keller said. "One way we do that is just wearing our masks."

Friendly confines

Friday was special for Shelton, who grew up in nearby Gurnee, Ill., and would be managing his first game at Wrigley Field. On Saturday, Keller is scheduled to make his first-ever start here.

“It’s going to be different because the whole thing about Wrigley is the fans and the atmosphere it brings,” Keller said. “It’s not going to be the same, but it’ll definitely still be a cool experience.”

Shelton said his first game at Wrigley was with his father, Ron, who kept talking about Giants legend Willie McCovey.

Building time

Keller did some fine work his last time out, tossing five innings of one-run ball Sunday to help Shelton earn his first managerial win. That does not mean, however, that Keller was completely happy with how he performed.

In fact, Keller mentioned two areas in which he'd like to improve: his four-seam fastball velocity and his percentage of first-pitch strikes.

After averaging 95.4 mph on his four-seamer last season, that pitch for Keller averaged just 92.0 against St. Louis. He did not have an explanation for why it dropped.

As for first-pitch strikes, Keller's percentage was at 55.6 against the Cardinals, down from 63.4% in 2019.

"I didn’t have my best stuff my last start, but I managed to get through it," Keller said. "Put in some work here between starts. Hopefully I can get some better stuff."

Birthday wishes

Shelton turned 50 Thursday and enjoyed a low-key celebration. He had breakfast with his wife (Alison) and daughter (Gianna) in Pittsburgh. When the club got to Chicago, Shelton had a deep-dish pizza delivered to his hotel room.

But his favorite gift might’ve been some new art for his office: a Pearl Jam mixed-media piece, multi-layered with song lyrics, acrylic and spray paint that local artist Denielle DeSantis designed with input from Shelton’s wife, Alison.
