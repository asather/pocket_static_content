Empty surgical waiting rooms prompt furloughs at Indiana, Mon Valley hospitals

Two community hospitals in the region furloughed hundreds of employees after the COVID-19-related shutdown of non-emergency operations emptied doctors’ office waiting rooms.

Indiana Regional Medical Center furloughed 200 employees through the end of May to “adjust staffing levels to match the current demand” for medical care, according to a prepared statement from the 166-bed Indiana County hospital.

Since Gov. Tom Wolf issued stay-at-home orders in March, the hospital has seen a 48% drop in patient admissions, 59% decrease in outpatient testing and a 58% decrease in surgeries.

“This was one of the most difficult decisions we’ve had to make in many years,” CEO Steve Wolfe said in a statement. “But we have to make changes in order to sustain operations and remain an independent health care provider in this current environment.”

The sharp decrease in patient volume and revenue also prompted IRMC to withdraw its final contract offer to about 380 unionized nurses, who have been without a labor agreement since Oct. 31, 2018. The layoff affects about 30 registered nurses, according to Annie Briscoe, spokeswoman for PSEA-Healthcare, a division of the Pennsylvania State Education Association.

No new contacts were scheduled, said Ms. Briscoe. IRMC nurses are represented by the Indiana Registered Nurses Association, which is an affiliate of PSEA-Healthcare.

At Monongahela Valley Hospital in Washington County, 100 employees or about 10% of the staff was furloughed due to government-ordered travel restrictions and shutdown of non-emergency surgeries.

Such surgeries generally account for a bigger share of revenue than inpatient care at community hospitals, according to a new survey by the Healthcare Council of Western Pennsylvania, a Warrendale-based trade group.

Mon Valley has 200 licensed inpatient beds and 60 of the furloughs were from the hospital’s Center for Fitness and Health in Belle Vernon, hospital CEO Lou Panza said.

“Our expectation is we’re calling everybody back. I want to see everybody back to work as soon as possible,” Mr. Panza said. “It’ll be based on patient volume.”

One sign that things may be changing: on Tuesday, Mon Valley had its busiest emergency room day in six weeks, Mr. Panza said.

Both Indiana Regional Medical Center and Mon Valley recently received low-interest loans from the state’s new Hospital Emergency Loan Program.

IRMC received $10 million, Mon Valley, $15 million at an interest rate of 0.5% for costs incurred between March 1 and Sept. 1.
