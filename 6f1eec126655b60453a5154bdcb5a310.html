Funeral held for man who went missing and was found slain in Fox Chapel

When Christian Moore-Rouse was only 3 years old, he proclaimed that when he grew up he wanted to change his name to Jesus. When asked why, he said it was because he loved God, Mr. Moore-Rouse’s sister, Dontesha Rouse, said in his obituary, distributed at his funeral on Wednesday.

Nearly 100 people gathered to mourn Mr. Moore-Rouse at Mulberry Community Church in Wilkinsburg. Although glad to have finally found his remains March 3 in Fox Chapel after he disappeared in late December 2019, many of his family and friends are still left with lingering questions regarding the circumstances of his death.

Some were answered shortly after the service when the Allegheny County Medical Examiner’s office announced Mr. Moore-Rouse, 22, died from a gunshot wound to the head and pronounced it a homicide. But no one has been charged in the death.

“The occasion may be solemn, but I want today to be a celebration of life,” Pastor Lawrence Smith said at the beginning of the service.

Following opening prayers by Pastor Smith and Bishop Lee C. Carter of King of Kings Baptist Ministry, as well as condolences from local dignitaries who knew Mr. Moore-Rouse or his family, like Pittsburgh Municipal Court Judge Kevin Cooper Jr., loved ones of Mr. Moore-Rouse offered reflections of his life and his effect on them.

They spoke of him as an intelligent, kind and charismatic young man, who volunteered for The Greater Pittsburgh Food Bank, Jubilee Soup Kitchen and Mulberry Church.

Andrew Coleman III, a minister at St. James AME Church in East Liberty, said he met Mr. Moore-Rouse while they were both students at the Pittsburgh Science & Technology Academy.

“Christian would just absolutely light up the room when he walked in,” Mr. Coleman said. “He was one of the most trusting people I ever met. But maybe it was his sheer level of trust in others that led to his downfall.”

After a eulogy by Pastor Smith, those in attendance were led outside to release dozens of white balloons, to symbolize Mr. Moore-Rouse’s passing.
