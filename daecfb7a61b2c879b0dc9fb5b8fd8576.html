PPG trying to 'limit impact' of COVID-19 on global operations

With an automotive coatings plant in Wuhan, China — the place where the deadly coronavirus is believed to have originated — and operations spread across 70 countries worldwide, Downtown-based PPG has been experiencing the economic fallout from the global pandemic longer than many U.S. businesses.

Company Chairman and CEO Michael McGarry said Thursday that the paints giant won’t know the full hit the company will take “for some time.”

Addressing shareholders during a virtual annual meeting, Mr. McGarry reviewed PPG’s 2019 results and said details about how COVID-19 has affected its performance since January won’t be discussed until a first-quarter earnings call on April 28.

PPG — which produces coatings for automakers and other industries that have experienced dramatic slowdowns during the coronavirus crisis — continues “to take proactive actions to limit the impact as much as possible,” Mr. McGarry said.

Asked by a shareholder whether PPG has applied for assistance through the federal government’s $2 trillion relief package known as the Coronavirus Aid, Relief, and Economic Security (CARES) Act, he said the company hasn’t yet but is studying its provisions.

“We’re confident our business is in good shape and we will continue to monitor the virus moving forward,” he said.

Its plant in Wuhan, which employs 35 and makes automotive coatings, was shuttered from Jan. 20 to March 23. PPG has 3,800 employees throughout China and all its facilities there are back up and running.

The company adjusted operations at manufacturing sites in Milan, Italy, and elsewhere as the virus raged in Europe and other regions. Some plants in Italy remain closed, PPG said.

In the U.S., its manufacturing sites are considered essential businesses that may continue to operate with safety precautions including split shifts and social distancing.

Its retail paint stores are providing pickup and delivery services.

Uncertainty about the virus will result in PPG taking a “conservative approach” to acquisitions in 2020, Mr. McGarry said.

Last year, the company spent $700 million in cash to acquire four businesses that make automotive and aerospace coatings products.

It closed on two deals in the first quarter: Industria Chimica Reggiana, an Italian coatings maker; and Alpha Coating Technologies, a West Chicago firm that makes powder coatings for light industrial applications. Financial terms weren’t disclosed.

PPG sales last year totaled $15.1 billion, down 1.5% from 2018. Net income slipped to $1.2 billion, or $5.22 per share, down from $1.3 billion, or $5.40 per share in 2018.

Shareholders Thursday approved the appointment of Kathleen Ligocki to PPG’s board effective immediately. Ms. Ligocki is the former chief executive of Agility Fuel Solutions, former president and chief executive of Harvest Power, and a former operating partner at venture capital firm Kleiner Perkins Caufield & Byers.

Earlier this week, the PPG Foundation committed more than $1.5 million to COVID-19 relief efforts in the Pittsburgh region and other locations where it operates.

The funding includes $275,000 for the Greater Pittsburgh Community Food Bank and the Emergency Action Fund at the Pittsburgh Foundation.

Other grants include $150,000 for Feeding America which supports food banks nationwide; $375,000 to the International Federation of the Red Cross and Red Crescent Societies for efforts in communities in Europe, Africa, Asia-Pacific and the Middle East; $168,000 to the Chinese Red Cross Foundation; and $520,000 for local organizations serving people at risk in communities worldwide where PPG has facilities.

The company also donated more than 290 gallons of anti-bacterial paint to Shanghai Tong Ren Hospital; and is doubling a match program for employee contributions in the U.S. and Canada to nonprofits involved in COVID-19 support.

Employee gifts will be matched at a two-for-one rate through May 31, PPG said.
