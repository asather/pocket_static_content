7 new COVID-19 deaths in Allegheny County; 4 Beaver County deaths were in nursing care

This story was updated at 1 p.m.

The state said there were 58 new COVID-19 deaths reported in the past 24 hours and nine of them were patients residing at Western Pennsylvania nursing or long-term care homes, according to the latest figures released Wednesday. Meanwhile, Westmoreland County has reached the 300-mark in its number of cases.

There are now 35,684 cases — confirmed and probable — in Pennsylvania and 1,622 deaths. The new cases are 1,156 more than Tuesday’s total of 34,528. 

In Western Pennsylvania, there was at least one new death reported in each of these counties: Allegheny (7), Beaver (4) and Butler (1) counties. Based on the state’s figures, the Beaver deaths were individuals who resided in nursing or long-term care homes. In Allegheny, five of the seven people who died lived in a care home. Kane Community Living Centers’ Glen Hazel facility also is reporting three new deaths since Saturday. Seven of its residents have died as a result of COVID-19.

In Allegheny County, there are 29 new cases, the health department said Wednesday.

There are now 1,088 cases — 1,052 confirmed and 36 probable — and 74 deaths — 59 confirmed and  in the county, increases from Tuesday when the county reported 1,059 cases and 67 deaths. Allegheny County has the highest totals in Western Pennsylvania.

 

Since the first case was confirmed on March 14, the county has administered 13,021 tests. Of its cases, 196 people have required hospitalization, with 76 needing a visit to the Intensive Care Unit and 47requiring mechanical ventilation.

Philadelphia County leads the state in cases and deaths, with 9,696 and 365, respectively.

On Tuesday, Dr. Rachel Levine, the state’s health secretary, said the department is making a continued effort to make more data available and has added a number of new features to its website, including a map of coronavirus cases by ZIP code and graphs with animated trend lines for each region of the state. 

Dr. Levine also said that she does not believe deaths are being underreported, especially with the addition of probable cases.

“We’re very confident in our data,” she said.
