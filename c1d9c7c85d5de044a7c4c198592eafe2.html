Joe Starkey’s mailbag: Is Eric Ebron a worthwhile risk for Steelers?

Welcome to Joe Starkey’s mailbag, where the Post-Gazette columnist and 93.7 The Fan radio host answers your questions about sports, life, Lucius Allen and everything in between. If you want to ask Joe a question, tweet him @JoeStarkey1 or email him at jstarkey@post-gazette.com. On to the questions …

Actually, we begin today with our intergalactically popular Twitter Poll.

Simple request, and people had two hours to vote: Grade the Eric Ebron signing.

Nearly 600 people responded this way ...

A: 25.9%

B: 61.5%

C: 10.9%

Worse: 1.7%

What we learned: That people can be smart, although you wouldn’t know it from observing them in other walks of life these days. I would rate this move somewhere between a ‘B’ and ‘C.’

On one hand, you have a tight end coming off double-ankle surgery who drops a ton of balls, doesn’t block, complained about his role in the Colts’ offense and had a major disconnect with the organization regarding his ankle injury (he said it was a season-long issue; they didn’t seem to agree).

On the other hand, you have a 6-foot-4, 245-pound mismatch maker, still just 26, a rare athletic specimen who can inflict maximum damage in the red zone and break a game open (he has at least one catch of 44 yards or more in each of his past five seasons). Just two years ago, Ebron had his one outstanding season — 13 touchdown catches working with Andrew Luck.

Also, the price was right. The Steelers got Ebron at $12 million over two years. You should have seen the reaction in Chicago, where the Bears had just signed a much older Jimmy Graham for more.

This was a worthwhile risk. Does that mean it will work? Not necessarily. I keep seeing visions of Ladarius Green, Donte Moncrief and other recent free agent busts.

Could I also see dynamic double tight end packages of Ebron and Vance McDonald? Sure, so long as both are healthy.

All in all, a worthwhile risk that might satisfy a need.

mgmervosh, @ariba21mgm: Do you consider Evel Knievel an athlete... Stunt man.. Entertainer.. I vote godfather of X Games... Athlete.. Be safe Joe.

Starkey: Thank you for the question, Mr. ariba21mgm. Evel Knievel was insane. I know that. An athlete? No. An entertainer? Absolutely. I remember watching one of his jumps at a friend’s house on closed-circuit television. Like a lot of them, it didn’t end well. I also remember coming home from school one day to find an Evel Knievel action figure with my name on it, complete with a motorcycle, courtesy of my Uncle Frank.

Like Evel himself, the action figure never seemed to finish a big jump.

Like a lot of toys I begged for, this one flopped. I’m not even sure it worked once. I would set up pathetic little jumps, like over a twig or something, and the ramp would crack or the cycle would veer sharply off the road. It was horrible.

But I did love Evel Knievel. I even dressed as him for Halloween one year.

Seth, @pfseth: Batting avg. isn't as valued as it used to be. But, a lineup with high averages is clearly a formidable one. Did money ball just give insight into stats that are cheap, or expose stats that don't help you win games?

Starkey: Is that a brain tease, Dr. @pfseth? I only know this: “Moneyball” was an entertaining book and an excellent movie, but it probably should have been titled, “Hudson, Zito, Mulder and some other cool stuff that helped the A’s.”

Those three guys (Tim Hudson, Barry Zito, Mark Mulder) went 57-21 when the A’s won 103 games in 2002. The other stuff was interesting and smart and somewhat cutting-edge, but without the three horses at the top of the rotation, it wouldn’t have mattered.

4Rodusky, @4Rodusky: What is the most iconic moment in Pittsburgh sports history for each team (college and pro)? Drafting of Mean Joe for the Steelers? Or the Immaculate Reception? For the Pens is it the drafting of Mario or his saving the club from bankruptcy? Dan Marino or Hugh Green for Pitt?

Starkey: Interesting question, Mr. @4Rodusky. Let us first get our definitions straight.

“Iconic” is defined as “widely known and acknowledged especially for distinctive excellence.” And “moment” is not a “game” or a “person.” It’s a moment.

So we are looking for iconic moments, which to me means moments that fans everywhere might associate with a given franchise.

The first two are easy:

Steelers — Immaculate Reception.

Pirates — Maz homer.

The next two, not so much.

Penguins — Maybe the moment Mario was drafted? Mario’s first goal? Maybe the moment they won the lottery for Sid? Mario’s goal against the North Stars in the Cup final? (Sorry, Neil Wilkinson.) Fleury’s save on Lidstrom to win the Cup? I’ll let you make the call.

Pitt football — They won the national championship in 1976, but the iconic “moment” might be Marino to Brown in the 1982 Sugar Bowl.

I have a horrible feeling I missed something. Tell me. Tweet me. I’ll change it next week.

But you do understand that a “moment” is not a game or a person, right?
