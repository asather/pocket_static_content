Cal U student sues school for refund over campus shutdown

A California University of Pennsylvania undergrad has become the latest Western Pennsylvania college student to sue her school in federal court on behalf of herself and others in seeking a refund because of the virus-related campus shutdown.

Ashleigh Coffman said in her complaint that California University's online instruction is sub-par and she wants her money back for the tuition she paid.

The suit, which is seeking class-action status, is similar others filed in recent weeks against the University of Pittsburgh and Carnegie Mellon University.

All of the suits make essentially the same claim: That students are being denied the education and experience they paid for because online instruction isn't an adequate substitute.

Ms. Coffman and her lawyer, Gary Lynch, said she paid her tuition for the spring semesters for an "in-person, on-campus educational experience" that the school can't deliver.

"She has not been provided a pro-rated refund of the tuition she paid for her in-person classes that were discontinued and moved online," the complaint says.

California University announced March 16 that all of its courses would move online by March 30 and said it would be "business as usual" for full-time students switching to online learning. No in-person classes have been held since March 6.

Ms. Coffman said the online instruction for some courses involves previously-recorded lectures posted on the internet for students to review on their own. For other courses, professors post notes for students to review.

The complaint says the online teaching isn't as good as the real thing and that students have been "deprived of the opportunity for collaborative learning and in-person dialogue, feedback and critique."

The suit accuses California University of breach of contract and unjust enrichment and is seeking a pro-rated refund of tuition for the spring semester.

Suits against Pitt and CMU, and other suits around the country, are asking for similar refunds.
