In new Woodward book, President Trump says he knew coronavirus was 'deadly' and worse than flu

WASHINGTON — President Donald Trump’s head popped up during his top-secret intelligence briefing in the Oval Office on Jan. 28 when the discussion turned to the novel coronavirus outbreak in China.

“This will be the biggest national security threat you face in your presidency,” national security adviser Robert O’Brien told Trump, according to a new book by Washington Post associate editor Bob Woodward. “This is going to be the roughest thing you face.”

Matthew Pottinger, the deputy national security adviser, agreed. He told the president that after reaching contacts in China, it was evident that the world faced a health emergency on par with the flu pandemic of 1918, which killed an estimated 50 million people worldwide.

Ten days later, Mr. Trump called Mr. Woodward and revealed that he thought the situation was far more dire than what he had been saying publicly.

“You just breathe the air and that’s how it’s passed,” Trump said in a Feb. 7 call. “And so that’s a very tricky one. That’s a very delicate one. It’s also more deadly than even your strenuous flu.”

“This is deadly stuff,” the president repeated for emphasis.

At that time, Mr. Trump was telling the nation that the virus was no worse than a seasonal flu, predicting it would soon disappear, and insisting that the U.S. government had it totally under control. It would be several weeks before he would publicly acknowledge that the virus was no ordinary flu and that it could be transmitted through the air.

Mr. Trump admitted to Mr. Woodward on March 19 that he deliberately minimized the danger. “I wanted to always play it down,” the president said.

Aside from exploring Mr. Trump’s handling of the pandemic, Mr. Woodward’s new book, “Rage,” covers race relations, diplomacy with North Korea and a range of other issues that have arisen during the past two years.

The book also includes brutal assessments of Mr. Trump’s conduct from former defense secretary Jim Mattis, former director of national intelligence Daniel Coats and others.

The book is based in part on 18 on-the-record interviews Mr. Woodward conducted with the president between December and July. Mr. Woodward writes that other quotes in the book were acquired through “deep background” conversations with sources in which information is divulged and exchanges recounted without sources being named.

“Trump never did seem willing to fully mobilize the federal government and continually seemed to push problems off on the states,” Mr. Woodward writes. “There was no real management theory of the case or how to organize a massive enterprise to deal with one of the most complex emergencies the United States had ever faced.”

Mr. Woodward questioned Mr. Trump repeatedly about the national reckoning on racial injustice. On June 3, two days after federal agents forcibly removed peaceful protesters from Lafayette Square to make way for Trump to stage a photo opportunity outside St. John’s Episcopal Church, Mr. Trump called Mr. Woodward to boast about his “law and order” stance.

“We’re going to get ready to send in the military slash National Guard to some of these poor bastards that don’t know what they’re doing, these poor radical lefts,” Mr. Trump said.

In a second conversation, on June 19, Mr. Woodward asked the president about white privilege, noting that they were both white men of the same generation who had privileged upbringings. Mr. Woodward suggested that they had a responsibility to better “understand the anger and pain” felt by Black Americans.

“No,” Trump replied, his voice described by Mr. Woodward as mocking and incredulous. “You really drank the Kool-Aid, didn’t you? Just listen to you. Wow. No, I don’t feel that at all.”

As Mr. Woodward pressed Mr. Trump to understand the plight of Black Americans after generations of discrimination, inequality and other atrocities, the president kept answering by pointing to economic numbers such as the pre-pandemic unemployment rate for Blacks and claiming, as he often has publicly, that he has done more for Blacks than any president except perhaps Abraham Lincoln.

In another conversation about race, on July 8, Mr. Trump complained about his lack of support among Black voters. “I’ve done a tremendous amount for the Black community,” he told Mr. Woodward. “And, honestly, I’m not feeling any love.”

They spoke again about race relations on June 22, when Mr. Woodward asked Mr. Trump whether he thinks there is “systemic or institutional racism in this country.”

“Well, I think there is everywhere,” Mr. Trump said. “I think probably less here than most places. Or less here than many places.”

Asked by Mr. Woodward whether racism “is here” in the United States in a way that affects people’s lives, Mr. Trump replied, “I think it is. And it’s unfortunate. But I think it is.”

Mr. Trump shared with Mr. Woodward visceral reactions to several prominent Democrats of color. Upon seeing a shot of Sen. Kamala Harris of California, now the Democratic vice-presidential nominee, calmly and silently watching him deliver his State of the Union address, Mr. Trump remarked, “Hate! See the hate! See the hate!” Mr. Trump used the same phrase after an expressionless Rep. Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez of New York appeared in the frame.

Mr. Trump was dismissive about former president Barack Obama and told Mr. Woodward he was inclined to refer to him by his first and middle names, “Barack Hussein,” but wouldn’t in his company to be “very nice.”

“I don’t think Obama’s smart,” Mr. Trump told Mr. Woodward. “I think he’s highly overrated. And I don’t think he’s a great speaker.” Mr. Trump added that North Korean dictator Kim Jong Un thought Obama was “an asshole.”

“Rage” includes the first-reported excerpts of letters Mr. Trump exchanged with Mr. Kim, and quotes Mr. Trump in his interviews with Mr. Woodward using expletives to defend their pen-pal relationship. Even as U.S. intelligence chiefs warn that North Korea is unlikely to ever surrender its nuclear weapons and that Mr. Trump’s approach is ineffective, the president told Mr. Woodward he is determined to stay the course and dismissively says the CIA has “no idea” how to handle North Korea.

“I met. Big (expletive) deal,” Mr. Trump told Mr. Woodward, waving off criticism of his three face-to-face meetings with Kim. “It takes me two days. I met. I gave up nothing.”

Foreign affairs experts say Mr. Trump gave up much — including by postponing and then scaling back the U.S. joint military exercises with South Korea that had long angered North Korea, as well as by granting Mr. Kim the international stature and legitimacy the North Korean regime has long craved.

Mr. Trump told Mr. Woodward he evaluates Mr. Kim and his nuclear arsenal like a real estate target: “It’s really like, you know, somebody that’s in love with a house and they just can’t sell it.”

Mr. Kim welcomed Mr. Trump’s overtures with over-the-top prose in letters. Mr. Kim wrote that he wanted “another historic meeting between myself and Your Excellency reminiscent of a scene from a fantasy film.” And he said his meetings with Mr. Trump were a “precious memory” that underscored how the “deep and special friendship between us will work as a magical force.”

In another letter, Mr. Kim wrote to Mr. Trump, “I feel pleased to have formed good ties with such a powerful and preeminent statesman as Your Excellency.” And in yet another, Mr. Kim reflected on “that moment of history when I firmly held Your Excellency’s hand at the beautiful and sacred location as the whole world watched with great interest and hope to relive the honor of that day.”

Mr. Trump was taken with Mr. Kim’s flattery, Mr. Woodward writes, telling the author pridefully that Mr. Kim had addressed him as “Excellency.” Mr. Trump remarked that he was awestruck meeting Mr. Kim for the first time in 2018 in Singapore, thinking to himself, “Holy (expletive),” and finding Mr. Kim to be “far beyond smart.” Mr. Trump also boasted to Mr. Woodward that Mr. Kim “tells me everything,” including a graphic account of Mr. Kim having his uncle killed.

Mr. Trump did not share his letters to Mr. Kim — “those are so top secret,” the president said — though Mr. Woodward writes that Mr. Trump sent Mr. Kim a copy of the New York Times featuring a picture of the two men on the front page. “Chairman, great picture of you, big time,” Mr. Trump wrote on the paper in marker. (Mr. Trump falsely boasted to Mr. Woodward, “He never smiled before. I’m the only one he smiles with.")

Mr. Trump reflected on his relationships with authoritarian leaders generally, including Turkish President Recep Tayyip Erdogan. “It’s funny, the relationships I have, the tougher and meaner they are, the better I get along with them,” he told Mr. Woodward. “You know? Explain that to me someday, okay?”

In the midst of reflecting upon how close the United States had come in 2017 to war with North Korea, Mr. Trump revealed, “I have built a nuclear — a weapons system that nobody’s ever had in this country before. We have stuff that you haven’t even seen or heard about. We have stuff that Putin and Xi have never heard about before. There’s nobody — what we have is incredible.”

Mr. Woodward writes that anonymous sources later confirmed that the U.S. military had a secret new weapons system, but they would not provide details, and that the sources were surprised Mr. Trump had disclosed it.

The book documents private grumblings, periods of exasperation and wrestling about whether to quit among the so-called adults of the Trump orbit: Mattis, Coats and former secretary of state Rex Tillerson.

Mr. Mattis quietly went to Washington National Cathedral to pray about his concern for the nation’s fate under Mr. Trump’s command and, according to Mr. Woodward, told Mr. Coats, “There may come a time when we have to take collective action” since Mr. Trump is “dangerous. He’s unfit.”

In a separate conversation recounted by Mr. Woodward, Mr. Mattis told Mr. Coats, “The president has no moral compass,” to which the director of national intelligence replied, “True. To him, a lie is not a lie. It’s just what he thinks. He doesn’t know the difference between the truth and a lie.”

Mr. Woodward describes Mr. Coats’ experience as especially tortured. Mr. Coats, a former senator from Indiana, was recruited into the administration by Vice President Mike Pence, and his wife is quoted as recalling a dinner at the White House when she interacted with Mr. Pence.

“I just looked at him, like, how are you stomaching this?” Marsha Coats said, according to Mr. Woodward. “I just looked at him like, this is horrible. I mean, we made eye contact. I think he understood. And he just whispered in my ear, ‘Stay the course.’ ”

Mr. Pence was the president’s one constant booster publicly and privately in Mr. Woodward’s book. When Mr. Coats considered resigning because of Mr. Trump’s handling of Russia, Mr. Pence urged him to “look on the positive side of things that he’s done. More attention on that. You can’t go.”

The loathing was mutual. “Not to mention my (expletive) generals are a bunch of (expletive). They care more about their alliances than they do about trade deals,” Mr. Trump told White House trade adviser Peter Navarro at one point, according to Mr. Woodward.

Jared Kushner, the president’s son-in-law and senior adviser, is quoted by Mr. Woodward as saying, “The most dangerous people around the president are overconfident idiots,” which Mr. Woodward interprets as a reference to Mr. Mattis, Mr. Tillerson and former National Economic Council director Gary Cohn.

Mr. Kushner was a frequent target of ire among Mr. Trump’s Cabinet members, who saw him as untrustworthy and weak in dealing with heads of states. Mr. Tillerson found Mr. Kushner’s warm dealings with Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu “nauseating to watch. It was stomach churning,” according to Mr. Woodward.

Mr. Kushner is quoted extensively in the book ruminating about his father-in-law and presidential power. Mr. Woodward writes that Mr. Kushner advised people that one of the most important guiding texts to understand the Trump presidency was “Alice in Wonderland,” a novel about a young girl who falls through a rabbit hole. He singled out the Cheshire cat, whose strategy was endurance and persistence, not direction.

The book charts the Trump administration’s failings and missteps on the pandemic, including the decisions and actions of Mr. Pottinger, Centers for Disease Control and Prevention Director Robert Redfield, infectious-disease expert Dr. Anthony Fauci and others.

Dr. Fauci at one point tells others that the president “is on a separate channel” and unfocused in meetings, with “rudderless” leadership, according to Mr. Woodward. “His attention span is like a minus number,” Dr. Fauci said, according to Mr. Woodward. “His sole purpose is to get reelected.”

In one Oval Office meeting recounted by Mr. Woodward, after Mr. Trump had made false statements in a news briefing, Dr. Fauci said in front of him: “We can’t let the president be out there being vulnerable, saying something that’s going to come back and bite him.” Mr. Pence, Mr. Kushner, chief of staff Mark Meadows and senior policy adviser Stephen Miller tensed up at once, Mr. Woodward writes, surprised Dr. Fauci would talk to Trump that way.

Mr. Woodward describes Dr. Fauci as particularly disappointed in Mr. Kushner for talking like a cheerleader as if everything was great. In June, as the virus was spreading wildly coast to coast and case numbers soared in Arizona, Florida, Texas and other states, Mr. Kushner said of Mr. Trump, “The goal is to get his head from governing to campaigning.”

Mr. Woodward writes that Sen. Lindsey Graham, R-S.C., suggested former president George W. Bush speak personally with Mr. Trump about global vaccine efforts, but that Mr. Bush demurred.

“No. No,” Mr. Bush told Mr. Graham, according to Mr. Woodward. “He’d misconstrue anything I said.”

In their final interview, on July 21, Mr. Trump vented to Mr. Woodward, “The virus has nothing to do with me. It’s not my fault.”
