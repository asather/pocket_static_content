Auditor general race still undecided; Lamb says results 'encouraging'

Pittsburgh City Controller Michael Lamb closed out election night with overwhelming support from Allegheny County in his Democratic primary bid for Pennsylvania Auditor General, but with mail-in ballot results delayed across the state, a winner is not clear.

Mr. Lamb, whom voters elected for a fourth term in November, received nearly 80% of the 139,511 votes cast in his home county Tuesday to become the party’s nominee for the Commonwealth’s top fiscal watchdog.

But results from the Southeastern part of the state, where Mr. Lamb’s biggest threat is based, had yet to report the majority of votes by the end of the night.

Gov. Tom Wolf on Monday signed an executive order extending the time frame for mail-in and absentee ballots to be received and counted in Allegheny, Dauphin, Delaware, Erie, Montgomery and Philadelphia counties, giving until 5 p.m. on June 9 for tabulation to be completed. Ballots must have been postmarked no later than June 2. A court ordered an extension for Bucks County as well on Tuesday, according to the Philadelphia Inquirer.

Statewide, Mr. Lamb trailed former Philadelphia Deputy Mayor Nina Ahmad in the 8 p.m. hour, but as Allegheny County tallied and reported results — with 99% in three hours after the polls closed — Mr. Lamb pulled ahead by nearly 14 percentage points overall with just over 48% of voting districts across the state.

“To see those kind of numbers come in a race where we were outspent 3-to-1 is encouraging,” Mr. Lamb said on a 10 p.m. Zoom call with campaign staff a news media. “We will continue to watch these numbers come in tonight and through the next couple of days, and we’ll see where we are. Right now I think we’re feeling pretty good, but we got a lot to still look at.”

With only 34% of the results reported from Philadelphia County just after 11 p.m., Ms. Ahmad held a 50% lead over Mr. Lamb, according to state election figures.

Ms. Ahmad said she was “feeling pretty good” as well about those figures, but that “it’s sort of a little too early to say too much.”

“We’re waiting for our Philadelphia numbers to come in, there were long lines in some places,” she said.

This is not the first time Ms. Ahmad has run in a statewide election; she lost a 2018 Democratic primary bid for lieutenant governor in a crowded field.

Mr. Lamb said that he was counting on voter turnout in his home county to be in his favor. 

“Allegheny County turned in a very heavy vote today compared to other counties across the state,” he said. “All we know is anecdotal, and it sounds like their turnout was low to moderate, and our turnout was maybe a little better than theirs.” 

Four other challengers  vying for to be the nominee remained far behind as the night closed, according to state figures.

With 48% of votes counted statewide, Christina Hartman, of Lancaster, pulled in 11.4%; State Rep. Harry Conklin, of Rush, stood at 10.2%; Tracie Fountain, of Harrisburg, at 8.3%; and Rose Davis, of Smithfield, at 8%.
