Brookline group to use nearly $20,000 grant to create community garden, apiary

An overgrown corner lot in residential Brookline could soon be blooming and buzzing with honeybees, thanks to a city neighborhood grant.

Brookline Together plans to use its $19,995 Urban Redevelopment Authority Neighborhood Initiative Fund award to transform a vacant, undermined lot at the corner of Jacob and Whited streets — just off Route 51 near the South Busway — into a community garden and apiary by next year.

“It’s very craggy, and there’s not an entry point from the street. There’s a lot we need to do to make it a usable space,” said Caitlin McNulty, president of the community group.

The project was one of 18 to receive the URA neighborhood funding.

The group plans to begin installing electric and water lines in April, as well as grade and remediate the 1-acre plot that has abandoned mines underneath.

The goal is to open the community garden plots for operation by spring 2021.

Trees will be planted around the perimeter “to make sure that we’re not disrupting the habitat of the animals living in that space and kind of create a barrier from the road,” Ms. McNulty said.

The project is seven years in the making. The urban beekeeping nonprofit Burgh Bees has a permit to install up to 43 beehives on the land.

The hives are expected to be installed by fall, and community members interested in beekeeping can lease them for $75 a year.

“It’s a particularly good spot because you have the [nearby] Mount Washington green zone, and you have the rivers, which have been a good food supply for the honeybees.There’s a lot of biodiversity there,” said Jeff Shaw, Burgh Bees president. “Their diets mostly consist of a protein and a carbohydrate — Protein is the pollen from flowers, and the carbohydrate is the flower nectar.”

Honeybees generally fly 3 to 4 miles in any direction from the hive, he said.

Burgh Bees has trained 1,300 urban beekeepers in the last decade, and the South Hills location will be its third, as the organization also manages apiaries in Homewood and Cranberry.

Ms. McNulty said that along with honeybees, gardening and food education will be a large component at the site. 

Scout troops are expected to use the space for insect observation and gardening merit badges, both she and Mr. Shaw said.

Teens from the Brookline Teen Outreach, another nonprofit that Ms. McNulty runs, will grow produce for cooking instruction or donation to local food panties.

The plot is located away from the neighborhood’s main business district on Brookline Boulevard. 

“It is right by the busway,” Ms. McNulty said. “We were thinking this could add more access for people. On Brookline Boulevard, you have options at the ready; on this side of the neighborhood, there’s nothing.”

Brookline Together is one year into its 100-year lease on the city-owned lot.
