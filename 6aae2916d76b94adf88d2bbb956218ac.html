A change of the guard? Downtown's historic Granite Building may be on the verge of being sold

In what could be a bittersweet moment, Holly Brubach may be close to selling the ornate Downtown building that she poured her heart and soul -- and money -- into for nearly 15 years.

After nearly a year on the market, the historic Granite Building, located at the corner of Sixth Avenue and Wood Street, is under agreement to be sold.

Ms. Brubach declined to identify the buyer. But according to local real estate sources, it is Hullett Properties, a Vancouver-based real estate firm that has developed a 26-unit condominium project in the Strip District.

Breanna Tyson, principal for Hallett Properties, which now has an office in Pittsburgh, stated in an email that “I cannot confirm this information at this time.”

John Jackson, the Cushman & Wakefield/Grant Street Associates senior vice president who has been brokering the sale, declined comment, saying only that the building was under agreement to a “very capable buyer.”

It’s not known at this point what plans Hallett Properties has for the slender but elegant 19th Century building designed in the same Richardsonian Romanesque style as the Allegheny County courthouse.

In the Strip, Hallett has developed 2554 Smallman, a mix of one- to three-bedrooms condos. According to the property website, 23 of the units have been sold.

It certainly would not be a stretch to think that Hallett may be considering the same use for the Granite Building.

Ms. Brubach originally intended to convert the nine-story building into condos after buying it for $925,000 in December 2005 during the first wave of residential construction Downtown.

But after the recession hit and two buyers backed out, Ms. Brubach changed course, dropping the condo plan in favor of a 104-room boutique hotel with three bars and a brasserie-style restaurant.

She ended up abandoning that $38 million plan after failing to convince banks to finance it during a hotel building boom Downtown. In both cases, she poured some of her own money into the projects.

Last August, Ms. Brubach, a former New York Times style editor, reluctantly put the building up for sale, saying that she was “really sorry not to make this work.”

She had hoped to leave her mark on her hometown of Pittsburgh by redeveloping the architectural gem, built in 1889-90 to house the German National Bank and to serve immigrants. It is located next to the tony Duquesne Club.

By December, Ms. Brubach had fielded seven offers for the real estate, with either condos or a hotel identified possible reuses.

Mr. Jackson said after the property was first listed last summer that it had attracted potential buyers from as far away as New York and, yes, Vancouver.
