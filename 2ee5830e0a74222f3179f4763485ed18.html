Cancer treatment: Bill lets patients, doctors decide on drugs, therapies

Cancer patients at the most serious stage of the disease shouldn’t have to rely on treatment drugs dictated by their health insurance rather than those preferred by their doctors.

State lawmakers agree and — in a rare show of nearly unanimous bipartisan support — have approved the Fair Access to Cancer Treatment Act, a bill that is expected to be signed into law soon by Gov. Tom Wolf. It’s a much-needed piece of legislation that puts cancer patients and doctors in control of treatment options.

The bill would abolish the often-criticized “fail first” approach to treatment for those with stage 4 cancer, the most serious stage of the disease. Insurance companies often force such patients to first show no improvement with cheaper drugs before moving on to other — usually more expensive — approaches.

Instead, health insurers would now be required to cover drugs prescribed by doctors for stage 4 cancer patients as long as they have been approved by the federal Food and Drug Administration and are consistent with best practices.

The “fail first” approach to treatment, also known as step therapy, has been roundly criticized by advocates for cancer patients as a shortsighted approach that delays other treatments and therapies for those most at risk. Patients diagnosed at stage 4 are those whose cancer has spread to other parts of the body or organs.

The bill’s prime sponsor, Rep. Kerry Benninghoff, R-Centre, said it was ridiculous to have insurers determine medical decisions. “At stage 4, the cancer is aggressive,” he said. “We want to give the patient the best chance — not make them go through weeks of medications that don’t address that person’s concerns at the time.”

Mr. Benninghoff has firsthand knowledge of the ravages of cancer. In 2008, doctors discovered his 4-year-old daughter had a brain tumor. She died two years later after numerous surgeries and radiation.

That was part of his motivation to put doctors in control of treatment, noting that they “don’t go (to medical school) to deal with legislative policies or insurance companies.”

At stage 4, cancer patients are literally fighting for their lives. They shouldn’t be bound by a treatment program or drug regimen based on a health insurer’s decision. State lawmakers made the right move to give treatment decisions to patients and their doctors.
