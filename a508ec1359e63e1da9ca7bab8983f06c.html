Funding will help city implement diversion program for those who commit low-level crimes

Pittsburgh will develop and implement a new pre-arrest diversion program for people who commit low-level crimes.

The program, announced by the city on Monday, will be paid for through a federal grant awarded to the Allegheny County Health Department in September. 

The pre-arrest diversion program is designed for people who commit low level crimes — like shoplifting or disorderly conduct — that stem from behavioral health issues. This also includes crimes that are related to poverty, substance abuse or mental health.

The city has partnered with the Congress of Neighboring Communities (CONNECT), and the two will collaborate in running the program.

The diversion program’s main goal is to ensure that participants are not entered into the criminal justice system.

"We know that cycling people through the criminal justice system does not improve outcomes,” Mayor Bill Peduto said in a news release. “Our goal is to continue to work with the community, county partners, public safety, and CONNECT to interrupt this cycle by implementing public health-informed practices like diversion." 

The program also will look to provide additional support to people who frequently come into contact with police, especially those involved in crimes of poverty.

Additionally, it will help people identify goals and take control of their own lives.

The aims of the program align with the public health focus of the Office of Community Health and Safety, which Mr. Peduto created in mid-June. The purpose of the office is to create a system where public health and social work experts can assist first responders in situations that mandate more long-term assistance or harm reduction.

The pre-arrest diversion program will be piloted in Zone 1 on the North Side with the goal of expanding it city-wide and then regionally.

The grant is paid for through the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention’s Overdose to Action program, which was designed to address the opioid epidemic. The county will receive approximately $5.2 million a year through Aug. 2022 through the program.

Legislation to accept the funds will be introduced at Pittsburgh City Council on Tuesday.
