Pa. Turnpike to raise tolls 6% in January, add 45% surcharge for most non-E-ZPass drivers

The Pennsylvania Turnpike’s baker’s dozen of annual toll increases will come with a special treat next January for motorists who don’t use the prepaid E-ZPass: a 45% surcharge for processing Toll-By-Plate arrangements.

The increase unanimously approved without discussion by the Turnpike Commission Tuesday for all motorists will be 6% — whether they pay using E-ZPass or have a photo taken of their license plate and receive a bill in the mail. Right now, it costs passenger vehicles $44.20 for a cross-state trip using E-ZPass and $61.80 for those who use Toll By Plate.

But in addition to the increase, those who don’t have E-ZPass and receive a bill in the mail will pay a 45% surcharge to cover processing the payment and help the agency balance its books.

The surcharge will not be added to six locations where motorists pay a separate fee: the Gateway Toll Plaza near the Ohio border; Beaver Valley Expressway; Turnpike Route 66 near Greensburg; the Findlay Connector near Pittsburgh International Airport; the Delaware River Bridge at the New Jersey border; and the Keyser Avenue and Clarks Summit toll plazas on the Northeastern Extension.

The change will mean the most common toll for a passenger vehicle will go from $1.50 to $1.60 for E-ZPass customers and from $2.50 to $3.90 for Toll By Plate, including the surcharge. For a Class-5 tractor trailer, it will increase from $12.20 to $13 for E-ZPass and from $17.30 to $26.60 for Toll By Plate, including the surcharge. It will also mean a bill for a $50 toll will include a surcharge of $22.50.

It will be the 13th straight year the Turnpike has raised tolls. 

It’s a big change for Toll-By-Plate customers, who had been paying the same rate as cash customers who already were charged a higher fee than E-ZPass users. The Turnpike initially suspended cash payments at the start of the pandemic in March to reduce contact between motorists and toll takers, but in May it decided to go cashless on a permanent basis.

As a result, the agency is in the process of laying off about 500 people whose work involved collecting or processing cash tolls. 

In a news release, CEO Mark Compton said the surcharge is needed to cover the higher cost of processing and mailing bills since it switched to all-electronic tolling. The agency would prefer all motorists use E-ZPass. 

“The new Toll-By-Plate rate reflects the higher costs the commission incurs to process the toll and collect payment — a pricing approach used by tolling agencies across the nation to cover the costs of administering AET systems,” he said. “This balanced approach allows us to maintain a lower rate for those choosing a payment method that is less costly to manage, while those who choose a pricier payment option absorb those costs.”

Overall, about 86% of Turnpike motorists use E-ZPass. Now, the 14% who don’t will pay the higher toll plus the 45% surcharge. Collecting that additional charge could be a challenge for the agency, which only collects about 65% of those mailed bills before sending them to a collection agency.

In December, the Turnpike reported that the projected toll increase for 2021 would be 6%, but if the state Legislature allowed the agency’s $450 million annual payment for public transit to reduce to $50 million as scheduled after 2022, those increases would drop regularly until they reached 3% in 2028.

But that was before the COVID-19 pandemic in March gave the turnpike a severe financial blow, dropping traffic by more than 50% and revenue by more than 40% for two months compared to the previous year. Traffic last week still was down 23.5% and revenue was off 30.1% compared to the same period in 2019.

The agency says it has lost about $160 million in revenue since March and has projected it cold lose several hundred million before traffic returns to pre-pandemic levels.

The board also passed a new operating budget of $426.2 million for the year that began June 1, down slightly from about $432 million last year.
