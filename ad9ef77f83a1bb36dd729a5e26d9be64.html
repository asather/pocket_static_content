Pet Tales: How to help dogs cope with the coronavirus shutdown

Happy, tail-wagging dogs are getting more walks than ever before, as shelter in place orders give them more time with the people they love. 

“This is a good time to enjoy your dog,” said trainer Deborah Miller-Gurchak, who lives in Peters with four Shetland sheepdogs who win ribbons and titles at dog shows and obedience competitions.

But while her shelties continue to enjoy their back yard romps and training sessions, other dogs are acting up and picking up bad vibes from owners  stressed by the coronavirus pandemic. Dogs that have been housebroken for years are defecating and urinating in the house.  Others are destroying furniture or their own toys. Canines that have peacefully co-existed for years have started fighting. Social media videos abound with barking dogs providing background “music” to Zoom and Skype conferences as their owners try to work from home. 

When life returns to normal and people go back to work and school, dogs will have to adjust again. Some won’t be happy about it and may experience separation anxiety. Four local trainers have tips on what to do with dogs now — and when it’s over.

Annette Sexton, Mount Washington

“Dogs are best on a regular schedule,” Annette Sexton said. “Try to keep them on their regular schedule as much as possible.” For the dogs of people who usually work in an office, that means sleeping most of the day — in a crate or in a designated place. 

Too late for that? Try to gradually ease them back to their “old” schedule.

Ms. Sexton advises leaving them home alone at times, even if you are just going outside for fresh air. If you have a phone call or virtual meeting coming up in the morning, hold back half of the morning breakfast kibble and scatter it throughout the house  so the dog can play “hide and seek” when you work.

She likes the Canine Enrichment Facebook page, which has lots of games and activities for dogs and people.

Ms. Sexton is still working in the Pittsburgh Public Safety Department, where her clerical job is deemed essential. So the daily schedules of her Beaucerons  Enya, 10, and Enigma, 2, have remained the same.

A longtime trainer who has worked at local shelters, Ms. Sexton does at-home consults. Most of her clients are referred by veterinarians but she can be emailed at inawolfshadow@yahoo.com

Marsha Robbins, Brookline

”My five dogs are just really clingy, now, and they are starting to get snarky with each other,” Marsha Robbins said. Of course that behavior doesn’t escalate into dog fights — not when the owner is a retired special education teacher who teaches obedience and therapy dog classes at Humane Animal Rescue.

When the snarkiness starts, she redirects their behavior by giving them a toy or something to do that is positive.

All of her dogs are certified therapy dogs and are used to seeing 200 people a week in visits to the University of Pittsburgh, Duquesne University and other places. 

“They are used to regularly meeting new people and visiting with old friends” and they miss that, Ms. Robbins said. “They are used to me leaving for a while each day, so I’m still doing that.”

Dogs that are now constantly with their people “are exhausted from following you all day,” she said, and that can result in bad behavior. Dogs need time to sleep in a crate or a dog bed in a quiet place.

Because groomers are not considered essential, “grooming and brushing is now almost a full-time job for me,” said Ms. Robbins, who adopted her dogs from shelters.

She said she picked mixes with long hair that is less likely to cause allergic reactions in people visited by therapy dogs. Her dogs are Maui,14, a poodle/Havanese; Sizzle,12, poodle; Margo, 9, poodle/bichon; Ming, 11, Pekingese/poodle; and Presto, 6, Yorkshire terrier/poodle.

Maribeth Hook, Point Breeze

Kobi,11, Frisco, 5, and Abby, 3, have been acting differently since the COVID-19 virus shut down the classes they enjoy at Keystone Canine Training Club and other venues. They are Australian shepherds, a breed known to be very high-energy. They need a lot of activity, including training classes.

The trio are “picking up on my stress,” Maribeth Hook said, running around the house more and begging for food. “Abby now goes nowhere without a tennis ball. So we are back to doing more obedience work.” 

Ms. Hook makes sure the dogs are still accustomed to her leaving them alone in the house at times.

“When I leave I give them treats — stuffed Kongs or anything that is long-lasting and safe, things they can’t choke on.” That makes her exits a positive experience for the dogs.

She recommends the SpiritDog Training website and Facebook page, which offer a free, 14-day training course. Also, the Behavior Vets Facebook page outlines schedules and things to do with your dogs.

Ms. Hook works with people who adopted Australian shepherds and other mixes rescued from a hoarder house by Animal Friends. The dogs have many behavioral issues but are improving rapidly, she said. She teaches at Keystone and works with the Speak for the Unspoken rescue group, which works with deaf dogs and others with special needs.

Deborah Miller-Gurchak, Peters

“I miss the people I train with at training clubs,” said Deborah Miller-Gurchak, but she’s still doing basic obedience training at home with her Shetland sheepdogs Heather, 14, Patty, 14, Sparky, 9, and Myrrh-Sadie, 18 months.

Ms. Miller-Gurchak thinks everyone should do basic obedience with dogs and take them on daily walks. She’s currently getting questions from inexperienced dog owners who thought this would be a great time to adopt a puppy. Now they’re having trouble, including a puppy who “nips” when corrected.  

She’s been helping people train their dogs for more than 40 years at her Hollybush Dog Training Services. She can’t do at-home consults right now but has information on the Hollybush Facebook page. She invites people to post their problems and questions there or call her at 724-942-3134.
