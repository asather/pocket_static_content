Law firm seeks to halt Gov. Wolf's order to close all legal offices

A law firm outside Harrisburg is challenging Gov. Tom Wolf’s latest order that all “non-life-sustaining businesses” must close to prevent the spread of COVID-19.

The firm, Costopoulos, Foster and Fields in Lemoyne, Pennsylvania, claims the order is “so broad and sweeping, it is manifestly unconstitutional and illegal.”

It filed a petition for an emergency injunction or temporary restraining order on the portion of the governor’s order that applies to “legal services” Friday in the Pennsylvania Supreme Court.

Closing law offices in the state interferes with the constitutional rights of Pennsylvania citizens to counsel and due process, the law firm said in its filing.

Because of the nature of their job, attorneys and lawyers can not work remotely, the firm argued. For example, if a senior citizen is admitted to the ICU as a result of the new coronavirus, their family should not be prevented from meeting their attorney to discuss a living will and power of attorney.

Already, the state Supreme Court ruled Wednesday all courts will be closed to the general public for at least two weeks. On Monday, the state’s highest court declared a statewide judicial emergency.

The governor’s latest order took effect Thursday evening and is set to be enforced starting Saturday. It came almost a week after Mr. Wolf advised all non-essential businesses to voluntarily close or have employees work from home.

“I had hoped for voluntary compliance so our public safety officials could focus on assisting with the crisis,” Mr. Wolf said in a video statement. “Unfortunately, we have not seen full compliance.”

All health care and social-assistance businesses, except child day care, can remain open. That includes hospitals and nursing care facilities.

Crop and animal farming, food and beverage manufacturing, grocery stores, beer distributors, utilities and most telecommunications businesses were also permitted to remain open.

Most transit systems — including air, water and bus transportation — are to continue operating.

Businesses that don’t comply with the orders could face citations, fines or license suspensions.
