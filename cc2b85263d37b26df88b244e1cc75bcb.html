Ross advocates want to expand program of students planting trees in parks

Fifth grade students at Ross Elementary School have planted 100 trees at the township’s Sangree Park, and Eloise Peet would like to expand the program to other North Hills schools.

Ms. Peet, director of parks and recreation for Ross, and Isabela-Cajiao Angelelli, founder of One Tree Per Child Pittsburgh, spoke to members of the North Hills school board Feb. 5 about the program.

“It has been really an amazing journey,” said Dr. Angelelli, a pediatrician. “All kids get to engage and leave a legacy.”

Ms. Peet said that three Ross Parks — Sangree, Evergreen and Denny — “have been hit by blight,” particularly the emerald ash borer, a beetle-like insect. The township is embarking on reforestation.

“We could keep every kid in school busy at Sangree,” she said.

Dr. Angelelli said that One Tree Per Child Pittsburgh started in 2017 and is based on a program in Australia.

“People that live in areas where there are more trees have better physical and mental health, and kids that go to schools where there are trees do better,” she said.

The organization has helped more than 1,000 students plant more than 700 trees in the region, Dr. Angelelli said.

School board President Allison Mathis said she chaperoned her daughter’s class at Ross Elementary when they planted the trees.

“My daughter still goes back to visit the tree that she planted,” Mrs. Mathis said.
