Virtual exhibitions? August Wilson Center and others show how to do it right

Virtual tours of cultural and historic sites are nothing new, but they have taken on an added significance in the COVID-19 era.

They are not only the next best thing to being there. They are the only thing.

The August Wilson African American Cultural Center has created its first virtual tours featuring two photo exhibitions that opened in the center’s galleries shortly before the pandemic hit. Videos, with music and artist commentary, offer insights into “Vanishing Black Bars & Lounges: Photographs” by L. Kasimu Harris and “I came by Boat so Meet me at the Beach” by Ayana M. Evans and Tsedaye Makonnen.

“These virtual tours get to the core of our mission to champion innovative young artists ... even at this time of social distancing,” said Janis Burley Wilson, president and CEO of the center. “The tour also serves as a cultural outlet for our patrons and, of course, provides access to a much larger constituency than can walk through our doors in Pittsburgh.”

In “Vanishing Black Bars & Lounges,” a New Orleans-based photographer documents the black-owned social spaces that are disappearing throughout his Louisiana hometown. “I came by Boat so Meet me at the Beach” is a collaboration between Evans, a performance artist, and Makonnen, a multidisciplinary artist whose works explore “the intersection of womanhood and race in America through video and images.”

Social distancing has stalled work on “A Writer’s Landscape,” a permanent exhibition on the life of August Wilson that is scheduled to open in the fall. However, more digital content is on the way.

Starting April 27, on what would have been the playwright’s 75th birthday, the center is introducing “Last Mondays,” a monthly conversation series streaming on Facebook. The first installment will feature actor-director Ruben Santiago-Hudson, poet jessica Care moore, scholar Sandra Shannon and actor Nathan James in conversation with artist Deesha Philyaw.

Here are some other virtual experiences waiting for you right now online.

Britsburgh: Also new to all things online is Britsburgh, which is offering a series of free livestreamed events using the Zoom app. Virtual Britsburgh starts at 7-8 p.m. Wednesday with “What's my cue?” talking scripts and Shakespeare with New Renaissance Theatre Company's Andy Kirkland. Registration required; donations benefit Pittsburgh food banks and out-of-work local actors.

The Westmoreland Museum of American Art: The interactive tour now has its own web page, where users can explore the museum with 360-degree navigation, from outside the front door through the 19th Century, 20th Century, McKenna and Robertshaw galleries and the summer 2019 exhibition, “Era of Cool: The Art of John Van Hamersveld!”  The Virtual Experiences page offers activities and museum staff talks.

The Andy Warhol Museum: A program added since the closing of the museum is not a tour, but an educational outreach that shares both information and pieces from the museum collection. “Virtual Warhol 101,” posted on YouTube on April 3, is an introduction to the museum’s namesake with José Carlos Diaz, chief curator at The Warhol and curator of the exhibition “ANDY WARHOL: REVELATION.” The “Warhol at Home” web page includes articles and activities, and The Warhol Museum YouTube channel includes t he one-minute video, “A Visit to The Andy Warhol Museum,” along with musical selections from the Silver Studio sessions.

More Warhol: To see Andy Warhol  as others see him, there is a virtual tour of “Andy Warhol at the Tate Modern” in London, posted on April 6. Tate curators discuss Warhol “as seen through the lens of the immigrant story, his LGBTQI identity and concerns with death and religion.” 

The Frick Pittsburgh: The Frick offers self-navigated tours with 360-degree views using Google Maps. Included are the permanent galleries, the first floor of Clayton, the Car and Carriage Museum and the grounds and gardens. Exhibitions include slideshows of “Gilded Age Fashion” and photo galleries of “Collecting Millet.” Frick Virtual Experiences include Curator’s Talks and online events that require registration, such as “Musings with Pittsburgh Ballet Theatre: Exploring Jewels,” combining “Maker & Muse” art jewelry and George Balanchine’s ballet “Jewels” (7 p.m. Wednesday; register here).

Sen. John Heinz History Center and Fort Pitt Museum: There are navigable 360 tours with pop-ups at the history center website, but for narrated, curatorial videos, head to the Video Gallery. There, you will find focused videos from exhibitions such as “#Pixburgh: A Photograhic Experience.” Narrators include senior curator Leslie Przybylek, discussing how early images of Pittsburgh reveal “Dogs as Part of the Family.” The Fort Pitt Museum’s website is a densely populated, photo-driven exploration of the Point State Park building that presents the story of the birthplace of Pittsburgh.

Roberto Clemente Museum: A regular feature of the museum website is the 360-degree tour using Google Maps navigation. The virtual tour gives you a sense of the space in the former Lawrenceville fire station. Museum founder Duane Rieder also shares stories about the Pirates right-fielder and details about memorabilia on Instagram.

Fallingwater: A mesmerizing 3D architectural video by graphic designer Cristobal Vila shows how each piece of the famous Frank Lloyd Wright masterwork was carefully placed over a waterfall to become integral to the Mill Run landscape. Since April 2, a dozen Wright sites began exchanging short videos of each other, so Fallingwater’s waterfall, in full roar, could be seen on the social media pages of the Emil Bach House in Chicago. The videos are posted at 1 p.m. Thursdays. Find a full list at SaveWright.org/WrightVirtualVisits.

Children’s Museum of Pittsburgh: The current website greets you with a video, “How do you say hi?” in today’s social distancing world, while activity videos for families are included in Museum at Home, There is a 2015 virtual tour, available on YouTube that is a step-by-step introduction to spaces and features in the North Side museum. The video includes ASL interpretation, and lets you know which space “tends to be high energy and loud.”
