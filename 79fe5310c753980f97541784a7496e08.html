Chelsa Wagner plea deal in Detroit means no felony, but case dismissal in dispute

Allegheny County Controller Chelsa Wagner pleaded no contest Thursday to one count of disturbing the peace related to last year’s recorded altercation involving her, her husband and Detroit police.

As part of the deal, Ms. Wagner will not face a felony count of resisting and obstructing the police.

But Ms. Wagner and the Wayne County Assistant Prosecutor’s office disagreed Thursday about when the charge will be dismissed and what the plea entails.

Maria Miller, a spokeswoman for the assistant prosecutor, said Ms. Wagner was sentenced to six months non-reporting probation and must pay $600 in court costs and $53 to Michigan’s Crime Victim’s Fund. Ms. Miller said the charge will be dismissed after the probation is completed.

But Ms. Wagner’s team said the felony charge will be dismissed “upon [Ms.] Wagner paying for court fees and costs,” which she did after the 9 a.m. pre-trial hearing before Judge Regina Thomas.

Ms. Wagner, in an interview, said the judge did not agree to the part of the settlement agreement that said her sentence would be delayed six months pursuant to non-reporting probation.

In response to Ms. Wagner, the prosecutor’s office put out another statement calling her claims “erroneous.”

“Please be advised that the case has not been dismissed by the court,” the office wrote. “Ms. Wagner has a six month period of non-reporting probation, where she must not have any new criminal cases and must pay court costs and fines. It is a plea under advisement and after six months the Judge can dismiss her case.”

The prosecutor’s office also provided a copy of the plea order in the case — signed by Ms. Wagner and her attorney, Charles Longstreet III and dated Feb. 13. The agreement includes six months non-reporting probation.

But Mr. Longstreet, in an interview Thursday, said that document was the original pre-hearing agreement between Ms. Wagner’s defense and the prosecution, and that he made a motion in court this morning to request a suspension of probation upon payment of fines and costs.

“The judge granted our motion,” Mr. Longstreet said.

Judge Thomas’s court is currently in session, and will not be available to comment until later this afternoon.
