'Maker & Muse' jewelry inspires poets in virtual reading

Six Pittsburgh poets, each inspired by an exquisite piece of handmade jewelry, will read their poetry on Thursday.

The virtual reading starts at 7 p.m. Thursday via Zoom and was organized by Lisa Viscusi, manager of adult learning for The Frick Pittsburgh. The event is free but you must register at www.thefrickpittsburgh.org/Event-Ekphrasis-Poetry-Meets-Art.

The program, which marks the end of National Poetry Month, is connected to the Frick show “Maker & Muse: Women and Early Twentieth Century Art Jewelry.” The exhibition features Arts & Crafts and French Art Nouveau jewelry and works by American designer Louis Comfort Tiffany. 

Ms. Viscusi is a former English teacher who led poetry and writing workshops with high school students and adults in Chicago.

“We were immediately taken with the word ‘maker,’” she said. “What a great way for poets to respond to these intimate pieces. We commissioned them to write a piece based on an object in the exhibition that they chose.”

Jamie Agnello, an actor, voiceover artist and puppeteer, wrote a poem about a ring designed by Tiffany. Veronica Corpuz, a creative writing teacher, drew inspiration from a ship pendant made of silver-gilt, copper alloy, abalone and pearl. It was made around 1915 by British designer Henry Charles Barker.

A silver and enamel cigarette case that shows a woman smoking was the muse for Jim Daniels, an English professor at Carnegie Mellon University. The cigarette case was designed by Alphonse Mucha, a Czech-born painter best known for his stylized posters of actress Sarah Bernhardt.

Karen Howard, coordinator of the Western Pennsylvania Writing Project, wrote about a brooch that features a winged sylph made of freshwater pearls and created in 1945 by French designer Rene Lalique. 

Ellen McGrath Smith, a senior lecturer at the University of Pittsburgh, founded the Writers’ Cafe Program at Pitt’s Writing Center. Her poem responds to a leather wallet adorned with silver and made around 1900 by an unknown German craftsman.   

Valerie Bacharach’s poem will focus on a pendant that features the three graces — joy, charm and beauty. The pendant is made of gold, enamel, diamonds and pearls.   

Responding to a visual artwork with writing dates to ancient Greece, where it was called ekphrasis.

“Poetry is a salve for grief and sorrow and a meditation, but now especially. I think people turn to music, poetry and art in times of uncertainty,” Ms. Viscusi said.
