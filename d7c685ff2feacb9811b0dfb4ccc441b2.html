Fitzgerald submits 2021 budget proposal with no tax increase

Allegheny County Executive Rich Fitzgerald submitted his budget proposal to County Council on Tuesday, a plan that — despite “very challenging times” during the COVID-19 pandemic — contains no property tax increase and includes funding for a new department devoted to early childhood needs.

Mr. Fitzgerald said his proposed 2021 operating budget of $942.5 million reflects a nearly 2% decrease from 2020 “to ensure the county’s continued stabilization with the expected impact on revenue.”

“You will see that many of our revenues did decrease this year, and understandably so during the COVID pandemic,” he said during the roughly 10-minute address to council members.

The administration imposed a hiring freeze in the spring and is proposing that it remain in effect for the first six months of 2021. The county executive said no layoffs are anticipated.

Revenue streams that have decreased because of the pandemic include taxes on alcohol, car rentals, casino gaming and hotels, as well as court fees, he said.

The administration is proposing a “slight” increase for health and human services and administrative services.

“As you can imagine, running elections right now is extremely expensive. All 67 counties in the Commonwealth have been given an unfunded mandate by the legislature to run mail-in balloting as well as keeping all the polling places open,” he said.

Additionally, the county will be establishing a new department called Children Initiatives that will be funded through “public and private partnerships,” according to the administration.

“I think there’s a lot of interest in trying to help and provide services and help kids be ready for school when they hit kindergarten and first grade,” Mr. Fitzgerald said.

A countywide ballot referendum asking voters to increase millage to fund learning programs failed in 2018; a subsequent report recommended that the county establish a department addressing early childhood education and after-school programs.

The administration’s proposed capital budget of  $127.6 million includes 64 infrastructure and capital improvement projects.

Mr. Fitzgerald said he attributes the county’s ability to increase capital spending from $100 million this year to the “fiscally responsible” decision to refinance bonds in May.

The proposed $1.5 billion special accounts and grants budget includes several federal Coronavirus Aid, Relief and Economic Security Act grants; the county received $212 million in CARES Act funding in March.

Talks for an additional relief package broke down in Washington, D.C., Tuesday after President Donald Trump decided to end negotiations with Democratic Party leaders.

“We’re certainly hopeful that they get back to the table,” Mr. Fitzgerald said, because “as the year comes to an end, that funding comes to an end.”

The county executive said the 2021 budget plan represents the 19th time in the past two decades that a millage increase has not been necessary.

Upon completion of budget hearings, council must pass a final budget no later than 25 days before the end of the fiscal year.
