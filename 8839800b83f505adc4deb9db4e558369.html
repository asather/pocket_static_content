Legislature approves natural gas tax credit to lure petrochemical makers to Pennsylvania

Years after Pennsylvania offered Royal Dutch Shell one of the largest tax incentives in state history to build a petrochemical plant in Beaver County, concerns about climate change, plastics pollution and the future economic identity of the region prompted a debate: If leaders had to do it again, should they make the same choice?

The answer from the General Assembly on Tuesday was a resounding yes.

By a bipartisan vote of 39-11 in the Senate and 157-35 in the House, legislators approved a multimillion dollar tax credit for future petrochemical and fertilizer plants that use Pennsylvania natural gas as a feedstock.

It is modeled on the tax credit written for the Shell cracker plant in Potter Township, but it incentivizes the use of natural gas broadly rather than just ethane, a co-product found in western Pennsylvania shale gas.

The Pennsylvania Department of Revenue estimates the program would amount to $22 million per year per plant in foregone taxes until the end of 2050 when the bill sunsets, which equals about $600 million per plant over that time frame.

Governor Tom Wolf has said he will veto the bill because he thinks such incentives should be evaluated on a case-by-case basis, his spokesman said.

If Tuesday’s vote counts hold, the General Assembly could override a veto.

In the Senate Tuesday, Sen. John Yudichak, I-Luzerne, said the tax credit “will support the development of a robust petrochemical manufacturing industry in Pennsylvania,” and replicate “the economic win” promised by the $6 billion Shell plant elsewhere in the state.

“Let us give every region — every region — of Pennsylvania the chance at an economic win,” he said.

Under House Bill 1100, a company would be eligible for the new tax credit if it invests at least $450 million to build a manufacturing plant and creates at least 800 combined construction and permanent jobs. An earlier version of the bill would have required a $1 billion investment and 1,000 jobs.

The bill also requires a company to pay its construction workers the prevailing wage rate and make “good faith efforts” to employ local laborers.

In exchange, the company would receive a tax credit of 47 cents per thousand cubic feet of natural gas it purchases to make petrochemicals or fertilizers in Pennsylvania, equal to up to 20% of its annual tax liability.

In comparison, the ethane tax incentive requires a larger investment for a larger estimated tax credit. An ethane cracker must invest at least $1 billion and create at least 2,500 construction jobs in exchange for a tax credit of 5 cents per gallon of ethane it turns into ethylene, for up to 20% of its tax liability.

At the time it was crafted, the ethane incentive was expected to be worth up to $66 million a year to Shell, or $1.6 billion through the year 2044.

Environmental groups and renewable energy companies oppose the bill, which they say should not reward the fossil fuel and chemical industries, at least not exclusively.

Rob Altenburg, director of the environmental group PennFuture’s Energy Center, said it would be fine for the Legislature to offer a tax credit to any company that invests the same amount of money in Pennsylvania and creates the same number of jobs.

“But what they said is, ‘If you are a petrochemical company and you make these investments we’ll give you a tax credit.’ Any other company? Too bad,” he said.

“They didn’t even consider that there are clean energy alternatives, efficiency alternatives, that could do a better job at bringing industry to Pennsylvania.”

The Consumer Energy Alliance, whose members include major oil and gas producers and users, said the bill “will keep Pennsylvania’s natural gas-driven economic revival thriving.”

At least one company has said the incentive would influence its decision to build a plant in Pennsylvania.

The CEO of Connecticut-based Elis Energy, Inc. wrote to the governor on Tuesday that his firm is exploring development of a methanol manufacturing facility in northeastern Pennsylvania. Its projected investment is $450 million and the creation of 800 construction and operations jobs — enough to qualify after the bill’s eligibility thresholds were lowered on Monday.

The tax credit “will significantly improve the economics of the project,” CEO Michel Goldschneider wrote.
