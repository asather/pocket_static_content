As stimulus checks start showing up, what will you do with the money?

Millions of Americans woke up Wednesday to find that the long-awaited stimulus checks being distributed as part of the U.S. pandemic relief package had been deposited into their bank accounts. 

President Donald Trump signed the unprecedented $2.2 trillion economic rescue package into law nearly three weeks ago that included direct payments toward 80 million Americans’ wallets as many of them have filed for unemployment amid the coronavirus shutdown nationwide.

While some residents are still waiting for printed stimulus checks to come by mail (a process that may be delayed after the Treasury Department ordered Mr. Trump’s name be printed on them), households who filed their tax returns the last two years and who have allowed the IRS to make direct deposits are seeing the stimulus money in their accounts. 

To check the status of your payment, use the IRS’ online tool, called Get My Payment, that launched Wednesday on its website. You'll need to enter your Social Security number, date of birth and mailing address in order to track your payment, the Treasury Department said.

Individuals will receive up to $1,200 and couples will receive up to $2,400 -- plus $500 per child under 17. Payments start phasing out for individuals with adjusted gross incomes of more than $75,000 and married couples who earn more than $150,000.

There were some hiccups as people rushed to check their accounts this morning: Some PNC Bank customers were having trouble accessing the website. PNC spokesperson Marcey Zwiebel told the Post-Gazette that the technical issues were a direct result of an “unprecedented volume of customers” using these channels to access their economic impact payments. Ms. Zwiebel said PNC’s tech team was working as quickly as possible to correct the issue. 

After weeks of anticipation, the funds became a reality for many who reacted on social media Wednesday morning to the relief.

While some said they were putting the money directly into their savings account, others told the Post-Gazette that they plan on making sure their check goes towards others in need during this crisis. 

Edison Norford said he plans to use his money to help out family and friends, while another Twitter user said he wants to spend his at a small business that is currently facing financial hardships. 

Another user said she was going to use her money for political purposes and put it toward former Vice President Joe Biden’s presidential campaign. 

One group that won’t receive stimulus benefits, however, is the majority of college students. Congress’ bill stated that parents or guardians will receive $500 per child under the age of 17. That means if the students’ parent or guardian claims them as a dependent but the student is 17 or older, the parent nor the student qualifies for a stimulus check — despite the fact that many students lost employment during the shutdown. 

Some students took to social media to express their disappointment through memes and GIFs about being left out of the stimulus package.
