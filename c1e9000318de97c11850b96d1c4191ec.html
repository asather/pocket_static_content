Allegheny County Common Pleas Judge Don Walko to step down in September

Allegheny County Common Pleas Judge Don Walko announced Tuesday that he will step down on Sept. 4, after more than 10 years on the bench.

Voters chose to retain Judge Walko, who currently works in the civil division, for another 10-year term during last year’s election. Pennsylvania’s constitution will allow the governor to appoint a judge, with two-thirds of the Senate’s approval, to fill the seat until the municipal election in 2021.

He said he ran to retain his seat out of indecision.

“I’m leaving not because I don’t enjoy being a judge. It’s just that I want to pursue other things while I can,” the 67-year-old said in an interview Tuesday.

Before he became a judge in 2010, he spent 15 years in Pennsylvania’s Legislature as a representative for the 20th District. He “saw a challenge to be met” in being a judge, and worked in the family division for eight years before he was transferred to the civil division. 

One of his future endeavors will be volunteering with VoteSafe Pennsylvania, a coalition of government officials — including all of the state’s living former governors — focused on educating voters on how to participate in November’s election.

“It’s hard to get it out of your blood,” Judge Walko said of politics. He pointed out that he has four adult children and is worried about the environment, civil rights and voting rights, among other things.

“I’m just concerned,” he said. “I want to be involved after I’m a judge in promoting things that I believe are in the best interest of our nation. It sounds corny, but it’s true.”

He cited growing partisan divides among Democrats and Republicans, a stark contrast to his early days as a legislator, when House members would debate with colleagues on the other side of the aisle and then “have a beer with them” later that night.

“We disagreed with each other on the House floor, but we did not dislike one another,” he said.

He felt frustrated and restrained at being unable to be involved in politics as a judge, even though he agreed they shouldn’t be political.

“I don’t think judges should be involved in politics,” he added. “You have to base your ruling on the laws and the facts, and if you don’t like the law — unless you’re on the Supreme Court — you have to go with it.”

He said he will miss talking to his colleagues about court cases and the camaraderie among people at the courthouse.

Much of what he wants to do involves spending more time with his kids and he will “see wherever the road takes me as far as politics and other things.” 

“The other thing now, going back into a nonjudicial post, my jokes won’t be as funny,” he said. “People won’t stand when I walk into the room. It’s a different world.”
