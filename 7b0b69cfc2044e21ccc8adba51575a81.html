Not lovin' it — McDonald's closes Strip District restaurant

In a land where a sandwich stuffed with meat, french fries and coleslaw reigns, the Big Mac couldn’t hack it.

The McDonald’s restaurant at 1630 Penn Avenue in the Strip District has abruptly closed its doors, leaving the owner of the property hustling to find a replacement.

“It’s surprising,” owner Dan Kamin said. “It’s an excellent location and I have reason to believe they did good business.”

The restaurant was perched at a prominent spot at the entrance to the fabled Penn Avenue stretch of merchants featuring the likes of Robert Wholey & Co., Pennsylvania Macaroni Co., Stamoolis Brothers Co., DeLuca’s, Jimmy & Nino Sunseri Co., and, of course, Primanti Brothers, with its famous sandwiches.

Mr. Kamin said McDonald’s gave no reason for the closing. It is the second McDonald’s to call it quits in the vicinity in recent months. The fast-food chain also closed a location on Smithfield Street in Downtown last year.

The Strip District McDonald’s, a longtime fixture on Penn Avenue, shut down long before its lease expired this summer. Jim Coen, head of a Strip District business association, said the restaurant seemed to have trouble keeping employees. 

While Mr. Kamin was stunned by the closing, he’s not crying in his Chicken McNuggets. He said brokers have been bombarding him with calls offering ideas and potential tenants.

“There’s no dearth of interest in the site,” he said, noting he has gotten more calls in one morning on the Strip location than he has in two years on a Kmart property in Parkersburg, W.Va., that he has been trying to sell.

Mr. Kamin said he sees opportunities for a bank, a restaurant, and perhaps even an office or another fast-food outlet at the location. With the lease not set to expire until summer, he has time to mull options.

“We’ll come up with something. The location is very good. We haven’t made any decisions,” he said.

And if for some reason nothing pans out, he has a backup plan.

“I’m thinking about opening a McKamin’s,” he joked. “I’m quite proficient at preparing oatmeal in the morning. Maybe I can serve oatmeal and coffee.”

In a neighborhood that delights in the eclectic, that combo just might make the cut.
