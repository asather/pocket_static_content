Allegheny College says it will reopen this fall

Allegheny College plans to re-open for on-campus living and instruction in the fall after an extensive review of strategies to do so safely during the COVID-19 pandemic, officials announced Thursday.

Students and employees were notified of the decision Thursday morning in a message from president Hilary Link.

“We have undertaken a comprehensive and extensively detailed planning process that will continue throughout the fall, including consultation of a wide array of public health and pandemic experts, to be able to create as safe an environment as possible on campus,” Ms. Link said. “We fundamentally believe that the fact-driven, innovative and collaborative teaching, learning and thinking for which Allegheny is known is needed more than ever to address such challenging global events.”

In recent weeks, a number of colleges and universities have announced with varying degrees of specificity their intent to bring students back to campus for some form of in-person instruction.

Since April, efforts at Allegheny have been underway to assess potential health and safety impacts to the campus and to those in Meadville and Crawford County. A range of individuals were consulted in areas including epidemiology, medicine, global health, governmental and alumni, officials said.

Allegheny College is a national liberal arts college that enrolls about 1,800 students, all of whom are in housing on or near its Meadville campus.

School officials said they are evaluating “residence hall occupancy, dining hall protocols, classroom occupancy, mitigation and containment strategies such as testing, isolation and contact tracing, and behavioral and travel modifications” to promote health and safety during the COVID-19 outbreak.

“As committed as we are to resume on-campus living and learning, and as confident as we are that we have a number of unique factors that allow us to do so safely, our return to campus must factor in realistic contingencies for the college to be nimble and responsive to ever-changing global health circumstances,” Ms. Link said.
