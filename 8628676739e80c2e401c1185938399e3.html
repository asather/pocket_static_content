Pet Tales: Tips from experts on how to cope with a pet's death

When a beloved pet dies, what do you do with the collar, leash, food dishes, bed and toys?

Keep those things to celebrate a pet’s life, says Joelle Nielsen, a veterinary social worker at Ohio State University. The university’s Wexner Medical Center VetMed department is one of the few programs in the country that has a full-time veterinary social worker. 

Ms. Nielsen, who has a master’s degree in social work, helps pet owners at the Columbus hospital deal with life-threatening and life-ending issues and illnesses.

She also suggests holding a memorial service, creating a scrapbook or collage and donating money or volunteer hours — in your pet’s honor — to a shelter, rescue or veterinary medical center. For more tips, go to vet.osu.edu/honoringthebond.

Dog trainer Penny Layne of Manor, Westmoreland County, suggests displaying dog bowls and other items on a “memorial table.” She and her husband, Shawn Murtha, recently completed training so that they can lead pet loss support sessions.

Their first Pet Loss Support Group meeting is set for 4 p.m. Saturday at American Legion Post 331, 216 Cavitt Ave., Trafford (15085). Future sessions will be held at the Sweet Buzz Bean & Bistro, 3353 Route 130, Harrison City (15636) from 7 to 8:30 p.m. March 15 and 29; April 12, 20 and 26; and May 4 and 18.

The number of professional veterinary social workers who focus on pet owners is rather small, but interest in the field is growing. Ms. Nielsen heads OSU’s Honor the Bond program, referring to the bond between people and their pets. Her duties include being with people and their pets before and during the euthanasia process.

Ms. Nielsen has developed and is coordinating operation of the Hospital-Based Veterinary Social Work group in the U.S. and Canada. The new organization is hoping to help veterinary centers expand social support services to more pet owners.

She notes that veterinary social workers are employed at Pittsburgh Veterinary & Emergency Center-North Hills, which is now owned and operated by Blue Pearl Veterinary Partners.

The facility in Ohio Township has two veterinary social workers. Its Pet Loss & Grief Support Group meets the second Saturday of each month. Sessions are free but reservations are required at socialwork@bluepearlvet.com. Its Social Work Support Line is 412-348-6515.

Ms. Layne,who operates Aunt Penny’s Pet Services, last summer started the Pet Prayer Blankets group, mobilizing volunteers who have made more than 1,600 no-sew fleece blankets for ailing and dying pets.

“We decided we needed to do more,” Ms. Layne said.

In February, she and Mr. Murtha flew to Florida for a weekend of training at the Two Hearts Pet Loss Center. They’ll be leading free group sessions, giving grieving pet owners the chance to “release emotions and go forward,” she said. 

Advice includes looking for a support group, which is what Ms. Layne and Mr. Murtha are offering. Go to the petprayerblankets.com and click on the “pet loss” tab to sign up for a meeting.
